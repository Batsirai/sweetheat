# The Day I Realized My Daughter Was Performing for My Approval

She was four years old and she had just done something funny.

I don't even remember what it was now—something silly with her stuffed elephant, I think, or a made-up song she'd been singing in the kitchen. But I remember exactly what happened after.

She looked at me.

Not with the open, easy face of a kid who just made herself laugh. She looked at me the way you look at a door you're not sure will open. Scanning. Waiting. Her little shoulders slightly raised, like she was bracing for something.

And then I laughed—I did laugh, genuinely—and her whole body exhaled.

She melted back into herself. The song started up again. The stuffed elephant went back to its important business.

I stood there for a moment after, thinking: *when did she start doing that?*

---

## She Already Had a Question

That look wasn't curiosity. It wasn't even shyness. It was a question her four-year-old self couldn't put into words yet:

*Am I okay? Am I enough? Do you still love me?*

She was checking her reflection in my face.

And the thing is—she probably does it every day. Most children do. It's not a behavior problem or a sign that something is wrong. It's what kids do when they're still figuring out whether love is something they *have*, or something they have to *earn*.

Most of them, without ever being told, start to suspect it might be earned.

They become very good at reading the room. They get a little sharper, a little more attuned to your eyebrows, your tone, the half-second pause before you respond. They learn what makes you smile and they file it away for later. They perform the version of themselves that seems to work.

And somewhere along the way, they stop just *being* and start *trying to be good enough.*

---

## The Research Nobody Wants to Talk About

Here's what developmental psychologists have known for decades: a child's core beliefs about themselves—about whether they are lovable, capable, and enough—are largely formed before they turn seven.

Not seven-and-a-half. Not in adolescence, when we tend to panic and start the therapy conversations. *Before seven.*

The stories they carry into adulthood—the voice that tells them they're too much, not enough, always almost-there—those stories get written in the everyday moments. Not the big ones. The small ones.

- The moment after they bring you a drawing.
- The face you make when they spill something.
- What you say (or don't say) when they strike out.
- Whether they have to do something impressive to get your eyes to light up.

None of us mean to do this. That's what makes it so hard to talk about. We're not failing our kids on purpose. We're just busy, tired, distracted—and nobody taught us that the ordinary moments were actually the main event.

---

## What She Needed Wasn't Praise

After that afternoon with the stuffed elephant, I started paying attention differently.

I noticed how often my warmth was *responsive*—turned on by something she did, turned up when she was being cute or clever, and turned down when I was on my phone or in my own head.

Which meant, from her perspective, there was a formula. Do the right thing, get the warmth. Fall flat, and the warmth is quieter. It wasn't transactional the way I'd feared. But it was conditional in a way I hadn't noticed.

She wasn't learning *you are loved.*

She was learning *love responds to performance.*

The difference is everything.

A child who knows she is loved—not for what she does, but for who she is—doesn't need to scan your face before she laughs. She doesn't need to wait for the door to open. She walks around like someone who already has a key.

And that security, that settled sense of *I belong here*, isn't just good for her spirit. It's what lets her take risks, try things, fail without unraveling. The research calls it "secure attachment." What it really is: freedom.

---

## The Shift

I didn't overhaul my parenting in one afternoon. I'm not going to pretend I did.

But I started doing one small thing differently. At bedtime, instead of reviewing her day—*did you eat? how was school? what did you do?*—I started telling her who she was.

Not what she did. *Who she was.*

"You're the kind of person who notices when someone is sad. I saw you with your brother today. That's who you are."

"You are so brave. Not because you didn't cry, but because you kept going even when it was hard."

"I love you because you're you. Not because you were good today. Just because you're mine."

Small sentences. Nothing grand. But I watched something happen over the following weeks.

She stopped scanning my face quite so much.

She started finishing her songs before she looked up.

She started telling me things with less setup—less performing of the story and more just telling it, the way you tell something to someone you trust won't need convincing.

The question was still there. Kids never fully stop asking *am I enough?* But she seemed to be hearing the answer more often, from somewhere inside herself rather than from my face.

---

## The Voice They'll Carry

Here's what I think about now, when I'm lying in bed too late and running through the day:

Every word I say to my kids today is an audition for their inner voice.

Not in a heavy, you'll-mess-them-up-if-you-get-it-wrong way. Just in a true way. The things we say to our children repeatedly—especially in the small moments, the half-awake bedtimes, the car ride silences—they get internalized. They become the voice that runs in the background.

The voice that either says *you're already loved* or *go earn it.*

Most of us had to learn, often painfully, that love wasn't something we could ever earn enough of. We spent years trying anyway.

Our kids don't have to figure that out the hard way.

We can tell them now. We can tell them in the car, and at dinner, and in the books we read, and in the moments when they're watching our faces and waiting for the door to open.

We can be the door that's already open.

---

## Say What They Need to Hear

You don't have to be a perfect parent. You don't have to have the right words every time or never lose your patience or always know what to do.

You just have to tell them, again and again, in a hundred small ways:

*You are already loved.*

Not when you're good. Not if you're quiet. Not because you made me proud today.

Already.

The world is going to try to teach them otherwise. It starts earlier than you'd think. By the time they get to school, they're already sorting themselves into enough and not-enough, noticed and overlooked, worthy and still-trying.

But before all of that—before the measuring begins—there's a window.

And in that window, the thing they need most isn't achievement or entertainment or even a perfectly structured routine.

It's to hear their own name spoken with love. Not tied to what they did. Just tied to who they are.

That's the story worth telling, over and over, until it's the story they tell themselves.
