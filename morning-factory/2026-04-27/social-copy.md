## INSTAGRAM CAPTION

She did something silly—I don't even remember what it was exactly. Silly kitchen dance, stuffed animal bit, something like that.

But I remember what happened right after.

She looked at me.

Not the way you look at someone to share a laugh. The way you look at a door you're not sure will open. Shoulders up, eyes scanning, waiting.

And then I laughed, and her whole body just—exhaled.

I stood there after she went back to playing and I thought: *when did she start doing that?*

That look was a question she didn't have words for yet.
*Am I okay? Am I enough? Do you still love me?*

She was four.

Here's what got me: kids don't decide to do this. It's not a behavior we need to correct. It's what happens when a little person is still figuring out whether love is something they *have*—or something they have to keep earning.

And most of them, without anyone telling them, start to suspect it might be earned.

So they get good at reading the room. They learn what version of themselves makes you light up. They perform the one that works.

I wrote about what I started doing differently—especially at bedtime. Not longer routines, not more affirmations on a chart. Just a shift in what I was naming.

Not what she did. Who she *is.*

## LINKEDIN POST

I've been in the business of telling stories for a long time.

But the most important story I've ever learned about isn't one I found in a boardroom or a case study. It's something I noticed about my four-year-old daughter one afternoon in our kitchen.

She had just done something funny. She laughed—and then, before she let the laugh fully land, she looked at my face. Scanning. Checking. Waiting to see if she was okay.

I laughed, and she exhaled.

That look stayed with me.

Because what I was watching, without knowing it, was a child learning whether love is something you *have* or something you *earn*. And she was already running the experiment.

Here's what developmental research tells us: the core beliefs children hold about their own worth—whether they are lovable, capable, *enough*—are largely formed before age seven. Not in adolescence. Not in therapy. In the small, ordinary moments of early childhood.

The face we make after they show us a drawing. The tone we use when they spill something. Whether they have to be impressive to get our full attention.

None of us do this intentionally. We're busy. We're tired. And no one told us that the small moments were actually the main event.

The brand I'm writing about today—AlreadyLoved—was built around one sentence:

*"When kids don't know they're already loved, the world teaches them to earn love."*

That's not a tagline. That's a diagnosis.

And it's why the work of telling children who they already are—before the measuring starts, before the comparisons, before the world gets to define them—matters more than most of us realize.

What we say to our kids today becomes the voice they'll carry. The question worth sitting with: which voice are we building?
