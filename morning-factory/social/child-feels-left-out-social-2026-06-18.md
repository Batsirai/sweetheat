## INSTAGRAM CAPTION

She got in the car and said, "Nobody wanted to play with me."

Four words. Matter-of-fact. The way kids say the biggest things.

I said "oh honey." Gripped the steering wheel. Watched her in the rearview mirror already staring out the window.

I didn't know what to say. And the things I *wanted* to say — I'm sure they like you, tomorrow will be different, you're so easy to be around — I've since learned those probably would have missed what she actually needed.

Because she wasn't just reporting a social event.

She was asking a question. The question underneath it was: *does this mean something is wrong with me?*

And she was watching my face for the answer.

What she needed wasn't a social fix. She needed an identity truth.

Something like: "That sounds really hard. I'm glad you told me." 

And then, when she was ready: "You know your worth isn't in whether they picked you today, right? You are already loved — not because you're chosen by the right people. Just because you're you."

That second sentence. I know it sounds simple. It is simple. It's also the sentence that, said a hundred times in a hundred different moments, becomes the floor they stand on when the lunch table gets complicated.

The child who knows they're already loved still feels excluded when it happens. It still hurts — it's supposed to hurt. But the hurt doesn't go all the way down to their core, because their core isn't built on whether they were chosen today.

That core gets built in moments like this one. In backseats. In the small decision to stay in the feeling instead of rushing past it.

You are doing that every time you pull up to pickup willing to hear what happened.

## LINKEDIN POST

There's a leadership insight I keep returning to in a parenting context.

When a child reports exclusion — "nobody wanted to play with me" — the instinct is to solve it. Call another parent. Engineer a playdate. Fix the social dynamic.

But when we move directly to fixing, we send an unintentional message: *your situation determines your worth.*

What the moment actually calls for is different. Stay in the feeling first. Then, when the child is ready, come in underneath the social situation with something that addresses the real question — not "did they like you today?" but "are you worthy of connection?"

That second question doesn't get answered by social engineering. It gets answered by consistent, quiet messages delivered in ordinary moments: *you are not defined by whether you were chosen today.*

I've been thinking about how early this dynamic begins. Children start building "internal working models" in their first years — deep beliefs about whether they are worthy of connection, whether their needs matter, whether they are enough independent of external approval.

Those models get reinforced in small moments. Including the ones in car pickups.

The parent — or the leader, or the mentor — who can hold that separation steady is doing something important. You can feel the pain of not being chosen *and* know that it doesn't define your worth. Both can be true at once.

If you're building that distinction in the children or people in your life — one conversation at a time — it's some of the most load-bearing work there is.
