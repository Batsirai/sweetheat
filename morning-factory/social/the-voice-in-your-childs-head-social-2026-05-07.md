## LINKEDIN POST

I want to share something from our brand's world that I think applies far beyond parenting.

There's a concept in developmental psychology called the inner narrator — the voice a person has running commentary on their experience. *I can do this. I belong here. I'm okay.* Or not.

Researchers who study early childhood consistently find that this voice is built, in large part, from what the people who love us say about who we *are* — not what we do, but who we are. The identity statements, not the performance evaluations.

What strikes me about this for AlreadyLoved is the brand implication: we're in the business of helping parents say the true thing about their child in a form that lasts. A personalized book where the child's name carries a story about who they already are — not who they might become, not what they might achieve, but who they are right now.

But the broader principle is one that I think every leader, manager, and communicator could sit with.

What do the people around you hear about who they *are*? Not feedback on performance. Not "good job." Identity. *You are the kind of person who notices things others miss. You bring something to this room that we don't have without you.*

The inner narrator doesn't stop forming at age 7. It just becomes harder to rewrite.

We're all writing each other's inner voices, all the time. The question is whether we're doing it on purpose.
