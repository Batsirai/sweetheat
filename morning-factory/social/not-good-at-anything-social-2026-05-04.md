## LinkedIn Post

A few years ago, I came across research that I haven't been able to put down since.

Developmental psychologists studying what's called "internal working models" found something that shouldn't have surprised me but did: the primary architect of a child's internal self-narrative is the adults closest to them — specifically, the sentences repeated in low-stakes, ordinary moments.

Not the speeches. The Tuesday afternoons.

I think about this when I hear parents describe the moment their child says something like: "I'm not good at anything." The instinct is to counter with evidence. A list. Proof.

But evidence-based responses miss what's actually being asked.

The child isn't making a factual claim. They're running a test: *Is my value conditional on performance? Or is it something that holds regardless?*

How we respond in that moment is data. It goes into the model.

The leaders I've worked alongside who seem most grounded — most able to take a hard piece of feedback, sit with it, and not crumble — almost universally have one thing in common: they describe having at least one adult in their childhood who communicated unconditional belonging. Not "I'm proud of you when you win." Just: *you belong here. You always have.*

We can give children that message intentionally.

It starts with what we say when nothing is going right.
