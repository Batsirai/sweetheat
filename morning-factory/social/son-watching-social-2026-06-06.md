## Instagram Caption

Nobody tells you that your son is writing a book about you.

Not in a journal. Not on purpose. But somewhere deep in the part of him that's still figuring out what men are — he's taking notes.

Not what you know. Not what you can fix. He's noting what you do with frustration. Whether you stay on the hard days. Whether you say sorry. Whether you turn toward him when he needs you, even when you're tired.

That's the inheritance.

My son said something last week that made me stop in the middle of the kitchen. He was talking about a friend who had done something that hurt him, and he said — he's seven — "I'm going to talk to him. I think he just doesn't know he did it."

Nobody taught him that. He saw it. And he decided, somewhere quiet in himself, that's what you do.

Your son is watching you be a man every single day. He's building an internal picture of what he's allowed to feel. Whether tenderness is something men do. Whether repair is available. Whether he is worth someone's effort at the end of a hard day.

You're not just raising him. You're giving him his first picture of himself.

Father's Day is coming. This is for the dads who are doing the ordinary, invisible, load-bearing work of being the kind of man their son will someday try to be.

It's worth something. More than they know.

## LinkedIn Post

There's a line I keep thinking about from a piece I read this week:

*"The skills are window dressing. The emotional pattern is the inheritance."*

It's from an AlreadyLoved Father's Day piece about what sons actually learn from their fathers. And it stopped me — because it's so different from how we usually talk about fatherhood.

We talk about teaching. Skills. Presence at the game, the recital, the dinner table. The moments.

What researchers who study father-son attachment find is that sons are not primarily watching for competency. They're watching for safety. They're running constant, silent diagnostics: does this man stay? Does he repair things when he breaks them? Does he have feelings? Can I bring him my failure?

The answers build a blueprint. Not for how to do things. For what a man is allowed to be.

I think about this in a work context too. The people around strong leaders aren't watching their strategy. They're watching their patterns. What do they do when something doesn't go as planned? Do they repair? Do they stay? Is it safe to fail in front of them?

The ordinary moments are always doing more work than we think.
