## INSTAGRAM CAPTION

She looked right at me and said she hadn't touched the markers.

Her hands were purple.

I almost laughed — but I stopped. Because her face wasn't defiant. It was scared. She was scared of what would happen if she told me the truth.

And that realization changed something in me.

---

We talk about lying like it's a character flaw. Something to stamp out, correct, punish into stopping. But kids don't lie because they're bad.

They lie because the truth felt like it would cost too much.

Every child is running a constant low-level calculation: *What happens if I tell the truth here?* If the answer has been — you get upset, you take something away, you get that look on your face — they start to manage your perception of them instead of actually talking to you.

The fix isn't a harsher consequence for lying. It's a safer relationship.

---

The sentence that changes the most: *"You're not in trouble. I just want to know what happened."*

Say it before they've said anything. Before the accusation. Give them the runway first.

The child who hears that learns something: I can tell the truth here and still be okay. This person is safe.

That builds slowly. It's proved in a hundred small moments — the times you didn't blow up, didn't shame, didn't make the relationship the consequence. And someday they come in and tell you something true that you would never have caught.

That's what it looks like when it's working.

---

*"What you say today becomes their inner voice tomorrow."*

The voice that tells them the truth is survivable — that voice gets built here. In the kitchen with the purple hands. In the car ride home. In the bedtime when you say: you can tell me things.

You're not raising a liar. You're raising a child who is still learning you're safe.

## LINKEDIN POST

Something I keep coming back to in the AlreadyLoved brand work:

Most parenting content about lying tells you how to catch it, stop it, and punish it effectively. That's treating the symptom.

The research points somewhere different. Children lie primarily to avoid negative consequences — they are calculating the cost of truth and finding it too high. The fix isn't making lies more expensive. It's making the truth safer to bring.

There's a concept we use at AlreadyLoved called "lowering the cost of honesty." It's not permissiveness. It's a different kind of structure. The child who has learned that their relationship survives their mistakes — that belonging is not conditional on behavior — has less to hide. They don't need to manage your perception of them. They can just tell you things.

What I find transferable about this beyond parenting:

In teams, in organizations, in leadership — we create the conditions for honesty or we don't. We either make it safe to say "I made a mistake, here's what happened" or we build environments where people manage perceptions instead of telling us what's actually going on.

The parent's version of that phrase — *you're not in trouble, I just want to know what happened* — has a workplace equivalent. It's not weakness. It's the structure that makes truth-telling possible.

AlreadyLoved's brand model is Seed (Identity) → Soil (Connection) → Fruit (Behavior). This article is one of the clearest pictures of that model in action: the behavior we want (honesty) grows from the soil we build (a safe relationship), not from the rules we enforce.
