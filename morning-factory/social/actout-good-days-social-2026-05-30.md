## LINKEDIN POST

---

I've been thinking about a pattern that parents describe all the time — and that most parenting content gets wrong.

Your child has a wonderful day. Best day in weeks. Everyone laughed. Nobody fought. And then, twenty minutes later, they completely fall apart over something trivial.

The conventional explanation is: overstimulation, low blood sugar, tired from transition.

All of those have some truth. But there's something deeper happening that changes how you respond.

When children experience a high of joy — connection, laughter, closeness — it activates a question they don't have language for yet: *does this last?*

A child who hasn't internalized a secure sense of belonging will stress-test it hardest right after they've felt it most. Because the more they enjoyed it, the more they have to lose.

Psychologists refer to this as testing belonging after positive arousal. Parents just call it ruining a perfectly good day.

The behavior isn't the problem. The belief underneath it is.

What that child needs isn't a consequence or a transition strategy (though those help). They need the answer to the underlying question: *I am loved not because today was good, but because I already belong here.*

I've spent a lot of time at AlreadyLoved thinking about this pattern — the way identity has to come before behavior, the way belonging has to be established before a child can regulate. We talk about it as Seed → Soil → Fruit. You don't get the fruit without the root.

The children who melt down hardest after the good days are often the children who feel most. The sensitive ones. The ones who laughed the loudest at the park.

They're not too much. They're asking the deepest question.

And the answer — repeated, consistent, unconditional — becomes their inner voice.

What you say today becomes their inner voice tomorrow.
