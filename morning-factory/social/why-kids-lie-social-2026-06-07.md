## INSTAGRAM CAPTION

She looked me right in the eyes and said she hadn't touched the markers.

Her hands were purple.

I almost laughed. And then I stopped, because something shifted — not frustration, but curiosity.

*Why would she lie about this? It's so obvious.*

And then the real question came: **what was she afraid of?**

Here's what I've learned about childhood lying that completely changed how I respond:

Children who lie frequently are not bad kids. They're kids who have calculated — consciously or not — that the truth costs too much.

They're afraid of disappointing us. They're testing whether honesty is safe. They're protecting the version of themselves they want us to see.

All of it comes back to the same thing: **belonging anxiety**. A fear that the relationship will absorb the truth and come back smaller.

So the solution isn't harsher consequences for lying.

It's lowering the cost of truth.

It's getting curious before getting corrective. It's receiving hard truths without the relationship contracting. It's a few moments — scattered across years — of saying: *you can tell me. Even the hard stuff. I'm still here.*

The purple hands moment became a gift. Because I asked instead of lectured, she told me she'd lied because she thought I'd say she was bad.

Not that she'd done something bad. That she *was* bad.

She was four.

And she already had a story forming about what her mistakes meant about her.

**That's the real work.** Not the lie. The story underneath.

We're building it every time we respond with grace. Or with rage. Or with curiosity. Or with correction that forgets the person inside the behavior.

Children who know they're already loved don't need to hide.

They can tell you the truth because the truth doesn't put anything important at risk.

## LINKEDIN POST

There's a question I ask myself when my child lies that changed everything about how I respond.

Not "how do I punish this?" but: "what did I accidentally teach her about the cost of telling the truth?"

That reframe comes from developmental psychology research that has been sitting in academic journals while parents are Googling "how to stop my child from lying" in frustration at 9pm.

Here's what the research shows:

Children who lie frequently are not children with character problems. They're children doing a real-time cost-benefit analysis: is the truth worth the risk right now?

If the emotional climate of the home teaches them that truth leads to disappointment, withdrawal, or shame — they'll sometimes choose the lie. Not because they're bad. Because they're rational.

What predicts lying frequency in children isn't the strictness of consequences. It's the warmth and safety of the relationship.

Children who feel genuinely secure — who have consistent evidence that the truth won't cost them the relationship — lie less.

For parents: this means the solution is not harsher. It's warmer.

It means receiving hard truths with grace when they arrive. It means getting curious before getting corrective. It means building a track record of: *your truth was received, and nothing changed between us.*

This is also, not coincidentally, what AlreadyLoved is built around. The belief that children who know they're already loved — unconditionally, before they've done anything impressive — don't need to manage our perception of them.

They can tell us the truth because the truth doesn't put anything important at risk.
