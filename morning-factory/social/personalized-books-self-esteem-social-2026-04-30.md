## LINKEDIN POST

There's a concept in developmental psychology called *narrative identity* — the idea that people construct their sense of self through stories.

For adults, we build these stories ourselves over time. For children under eight, the stories are built for them — by what they hear, what's repeated, what gets said at the most emotionally receptive moments of their day.

Bedtime is the most receptive moment.

Which is why what a child hears at bedtime — not just once, but again and again — matters more than most parents realize.

AlreadyLoved personalized children's books are built around a single premise: a child who hears their name in a story of belonging, night after night, builds a different self-concept than one who doesn't.

Not because the book is magic. Because identity is built by repetition. Because the brain of a three-year-old is encoding its first story about who they are — and a book that says "your name, your self, your worth" is one clear, consistent voice in that encoding.

What we've seen: children with personalized books ask for them more. Repeat phrases from them unprompted. Use the book as a comfort object in ways that aren't typical of other favorites.

And slowly, quietly: they start to say things like "I can do this" before a parent gets the chance.
