## INSTAGRAM CAPTION

There are things I've been trying to get into my kids before the world fully gets in.

Not lessons. Not rules. Something older than that.

Things I want sitting so deep in them that they feel like instinct rather than something they were taught.

The one I come back to most:

**They were loved before they did anything to earn it.**

The world is going to teach them the opposite. Quietly, consistently, in a hundred ways they won't even notice. Love as a reward. Belonging as something you perform your way into. Worth as a measure of achievement.

I want something in them that is older than that message. Something that got there first.

So that when the world says *prove yourself* — they have something inside that already knows: *I don't have to.*

I wrote down the full list. The things I keep trying to say in every possible language. In how I respond when they fail. In what I say when I tuck them in. In the way I come back after I've lost my patience.

Some of it is simple. Some of it took me a long time to believe for myself.

All of it comes back to the same place:

*You were already mine. Before the grade, before the game, before you'd figured out who you are.*

*You are already loved.*

That's the whole thing. Everything else is downstream of that one.

## LINKEDIN POST

I've been thinking about what I want inside my children before the world fully gets in.

Not skills. Not values in the abstract. Something more foundational.

The thing I come back to most: I want them to know they were loved before they did anything to earn it.

In leadership, we talk a lot about intrinsic motivation — the difference between people who do excellent work because they love the work, and people who do excellent work because they're afraid of what happens if they don't.

The second group often performs just as well, for a while. Sometimes better. But there's a cost. An internal engine always running: *am I still okay, am I still okay.*

Children who grow up believing love is conditional develop the same pattern. They learn to read the room very well. They become very good at becoming whoever the room needs them to be.

It's not a character flaw. It's a rational adaptation to an environment where approval was the signal they had to track.

I think about this as a parent, and also as someone who leads people.

The most creative, resilient, and honest people I know share something: they seem to have a stable floor. Something that doesn't shift when they fail or when they're criticized. They can hear hard feedback without it threatening something core.

I believe that floor gets built early. In small, repeated moments. In what happens after a child fails. In whether the relationship contracts or holds.

What I want my kids to carry: the knowledge that they were already enough before they impressed anyone.

What I want the people I work with to feel: the same thing, honestly.
