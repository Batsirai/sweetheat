## INSTAGRAM CAPTION

---

She was four the first year she really paid attention to fireworks.

She grabbed my arm. Pulled me close. Watched the sky like it was telling her something.

She wasn't scared. She was *receiving* them. Like they were for her.

And I realized: to a child, they kind of are.

Before kids understand anything about 1776 or liberty or what the word "independence" even means — they feel something on this holiday. They feel the largeness of it. The gathering. The sense that something is being shared and they are included in the sharing.

They feel, in other words, like they belong somewhere.

That's what the research keeps coming back to, in all kinds of ways: belonging is not something children figure out later. It starts in the arms. The kitchen. The car ride where you explain something and they trust that you know what you're talking about. The bedtime where you come back.

By the time a child stands in a backyard watching fireworks, she's been building her sense of belonging for years. She doesn't know that. She just knows whether she feels like she has a place.

And here's the thing I think about a lot:

The big belonging — to a place, a country, an idea — can only be felt by a child who already has the smaller belonging. The one that's just for her.

The one that says: I know your name. You are mine. Nothing about this changes.

So this Fourth of July, before you explain what happened in 1776 or recite the Preamble —

Lean close. Wait for a good one. And tell her she belongs here.

She'll understand everything important about independence from that one sentence.

## LINKEDIN POST

---

There's a developmental insight underneath all of the July 4th noise that I keep thinking about.

Children experience belonging before they can articulate it.

Before a child understands what independence means, she understands whether she has a place. Whether she's included. Whether this gathering is also hers.

Research in attachment theory is clear about what happens next: the children who have that felt sense of belonging early — not earned, not conditional, just received — are the ones who can reach toward larger belongings later. Community. Country. World.

The architecture of a child's civic identity starts at the table. In the specific belonging of being known by specific people.

This is why the AlreadyLoved model is structured the way it is: Seed (Identity) → Soil (Connection) → Fruit (Behavior). You cannot build a child's civic participation by teaching participation. You build it by building the person. The child who knows she matters — not because she achieves but because she is — grows into someone who cares about whether other people matter too.

The child who's still trying to earn her seat doesn't have that kind of surplus. She's spending everything she has on the closer question.

This Fourth of July, the most important thing parents can do isn't a history lesson. It's making sure that when the fireworks start, every child in their care knows she belongs in the audience.

Because the children who grow up knowing they belong somewhere become the adults who make room for other people to belong somewhere.

That's the thing worth celebrating.
