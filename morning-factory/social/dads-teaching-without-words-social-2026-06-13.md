## LINKEDIN POST

Father's Day is next Sunday.

Most of the content around it goes one of two directions: celebration or prescription. Either "great dads are amazing" or "here's what to do to be a better dad."

Neither one names what's actually happening in a household where a father consistently shows up.

Dads teach through the unconscious curriculum. Not the speeches. The texture of ordinary days.

What he models under pressure. How he handles it when he gets it wrong. Whether he lets his kids see him be moved by things. Whether he comes home when he says he will.

The child is doing something that developmental researchers call identification modeling — building an internal picture of what it means to be a person in the world. That picture is largely drawn from the parent in the room.

A dad who repairs after conflict teaches something that behavioral charts can't: relationships survive hard things. Which becomes, in adulthood, an inner voice that says "I can get this wrong and still be loved."

A dad who shows up consistently — not perfectly, but reliably — teaches unconditional worth: you are worth coming home to.

Those aren't lessons taught in a conversation. They accumulate over years of ordinary days.

What you say today becomes their inner voice tomorrow.
