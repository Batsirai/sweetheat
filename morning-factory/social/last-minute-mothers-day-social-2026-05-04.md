## LinkedIn Post

Seven years ago, I heard a concept that changed how I thought about parenting.

A researcher described what she called "the evidence collection phase" — the window between roughly ages 2 and 8 when children are actively building a self. They're not passive. They're gathering evidence about whether they belong, whether they matter, whether they're loved without conditions.

Most of that evidence comes from the people closest to them.

The words at bedtime. The stories they hear repeated. The things adults say when nothing in particular is happening.

This Mother's Day, I've been thinking about that framing. Because the most meaningful gift a parent can give their child isn't tied to an occasion. It's the message their child carries when no one is watching.

*You were wanted before you were born. You are loved before you do anything. Nothing changes that.*

At AlreadyLoved, we build personalized books around that idea. Books that call children by name in stories about who they already are — not stories about what they might achieve.

The children who know they are already loved don't have to spend their lives trying to earn it.

That's the gift worth giving.
