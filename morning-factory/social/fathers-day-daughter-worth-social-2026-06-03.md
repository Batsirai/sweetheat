## Instagram Caption

He was just washing the dishes.

He didn't know his daughter was sitting on the kitchen floor behind him, watching him move around the space her mom hadn't had a chance to clean up after a late night.

She was four. She had crayons and a notebook. She was supposed to be drawing.

But mostly, she was filing.

—

I think about this a lot. The way kids are always watching — not in a dramatic way, but in that quiet, constant way children absorb everything around them. Building a picture of the world. A picture of love. A picture of what they're worth.

A daughter's sense of her own worth is built, largely, from ordinary moments with her father.

Not the speeches. Not "I'm so proud of you." Not the talk you're saving for when she's old enough. Those things matter — but they're not where the picture forms.

The picture forms in whether he looks up when she walks in. Whether he stays when she's difficult. Whether she watches him be wrong about something and then sees what he does with it.

She is quietly doing the math. *Is this what love looks like? Is this what I deserve?*

## LinkedIn Post

There's a piece of research I keep coming back to.

Girls who grow up with fathers who are present — not perfect, just present — tend to carry something into adulthood. Not an immunity to hard things. Something more like an internal reference point. A voice that says: *you are the kind of person who gets loved like this.*

That voice is formed from evidence. From thousands of small, ordinary, unscripted moments that accumulate over years into a picture. And it becomes — sometimes decades later, in rooms the father never saw — the compass a woman uses to recognize love, and to recognize its absence.

The most consequential father-daughter teaching doesn't happen in the speeches. It happens in whether he looks up when she walks in. Whether he stays when she's being difficult. Whether she ever watches him wrong about something, and what he does with that.

As Father's Day approaches, I find myself thinking less about gift guides and more about formation. About the ordinary Tuesday evenings that most fathers don't realize they're building anything in at all.

You don't have to be a perfect father. The research doesn't ask for that, and neither does she.

She just needs a man who keeps showing up as someone she can learn to believe in. A man whose presence says, without words: *you were always already worth something.*
