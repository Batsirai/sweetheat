## INSTAGRAM CAPTION

It was 7:14 in the morning.

The Mother's Day cards were still on the counter, a little bent at the corners. My coffee went cold because I forgot to drink it. My youngest was narrating what the dog was thinking. My oldest came downstairs, poured cereal, sat across from me, and said — without looking up —

"I think I'm a pretty good big brother."

Not to me. Not as a question. Just a fact he'd apparently decided.

---

I've been thinking about it since.

Here's what nobody really says about Mother's Day: the most important part isn't what you receive.

It's what your kids show you about who they're becoming.

My son's card said "you teach me how to be kind." My daughter made me a flower out of coffee filters — spent an hour on it — and said when she handed it over: "I made it last a long time because you last a long time."

They were telling me who they are. And I got so caught up in the smoke alarm and the photos and the dog stealing French toast that I didn't say the true thing back.

Not thank-you. Not *this is so beautiful.* The one that names what I saw.

*You are someone who knows how to love people.* I meant to say that to my son.

*You believe things are worth lasting.* I meant to say that to my daughter.

---

This morning, the ordinary is back. The lunches. The backpacks. The Wednesday feeling.

And it turns out that's exactly when to say it.

"I've been thinking about what you said this morning. About being a good big brother. I want you to know — you're right. I've watched you become that for years."

He'll shrug. He's nine.

But he'll file it somewhere.

And someday, when the world asks him who he is, something his mother said on a random Tuesday over cereal is going to show up.

## LINKEDIN POST

Two days ago, a nine-year-old sat down at the kitchen counter, poured himself cereal, and said — without prompting, without looking up — "I think I'm a pretty good big brother."

Not to his mother. Not as a question.

Just a thing he'd decided about himself.

---

Here's what strikes me about that moment, and why I think it matters beyond parenting:

We spend a lot of time thinking about what we say *to* the people we're responsible for — our kids, our team members, our employees. The feedback. The direction. The praise for what went well.

But developmental research points to something different as the real shaper of identity. Not what we say *to* people. What we say about who they already *are*.

Children who grow up hearing their identity named — not praised for achievement, but told the truth about who they are — develop a settled inner foundation. A voice that belongs to them. That shows up when things are hard, when they fail, when the room isn't on their side.

It's the same in organizations. The people who grow most visibly aren't always the ones with the most capable manager. They're often the ones with a manager who said, at a key moment: *I see something in you. Not because of what you did last quarter. Just because of who you are.*

Identity before performance. Connection before correction.

---

This week, I've been thinking about what it means to give the people I love that sentence.

Not on the holiday. Not when they've earned it.

On the Tuesday morning after. When the ordinary comes back. When nobody's performing.

That's when it means the most.
