## INSTAGRAM CAPTION

The bedtime routine I built in October is completely gone.

The sun doesn't go down until 8:30. Someone needs a bug jar. Someone decided 7pm is actually snack time. The toddler napped for eleven minutes in the car at exactly the wrong moment.

Every summer I lose the routine. And every summer I spend about a week feeling guilty about it, like I've failed at the one thing I was doing right.

Here's what I've figured out: I was losing the wrong thing.

Your child doesn't feel safe at bedtime because of the schedule. They feel safe because of the *ritual.*

A schedule is a time. A ritual is a repeated act of coming together — the bath, the book, the specific song you've been singing since they were five months old and can't stop now. The goodnight they expect. The way you say their name when you leave the room.

When summer breaks the schedule, you lose the time. You don't have to lose the ritual.

The three things worth protecting:

A transition marker — something that says "day is over, we're moving toward sleep." Doesn't matter what it is as long as it's consistent.

A connection moment — five minutes of full presence. Not your distracted presence. Real, eyes-on-her presence. The check-in. The story. The thing she wanted to tell you but didn't get to earlier.

The goodbye — same words, same gesture, every single night. She might seem too old for this. She is not.

The elaborate October routine was scaffolding around those three things. The scaffolding can change. The foundation can stay.

## LINKEDIN POST

Something interesting happens every June in early childhood settings: the routines that worked beautifully in the fall suddenly stop working, and parents arrive exhausted, confused, and convinced they've done something wrong.

They haven't. Summer happened.

Here's what the research actually says about why bedtime routines work — and it's not the 7pm start time.

Children's nervous systems are pattern-seeking. What settles a child at bedtime isn't a precise schedule; it's a reliable *sequence.* The predictable coming-together that happens before sleep. The transition signal, the connection moment, the goodbye ritual. The feeling of: *we always end the day like this.*

When summer disrupts the schedule, the sequence can still be preserved. The three elements that matter most:

**1. A transition marker** — something consistent that signals "day is over." What it is matters less than the fact that it always happens.

**2. A connection moment** — 5-10 minutes of full parental presence before sleep. Attachment research is consistent here: children fall asleep more easily when their attachment system is settled. Presence isn't just emotionally helpful. It's physiologically regulating.

**3. The goodbye ritual** — specific, consistent language or gesture that closes the night. This one persists further into childhood than most parents expect.

The elaborate October routine was scaffolding around these three things. The scaffolding can change. The foundation — repeated acts of coming together — can stay.

This is what we mean at AlreadyLoved by "connection is the soil." Not schedule adherence. The feeling of: *we always come back to each other.*
