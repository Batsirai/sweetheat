## INSTAGRAM CAPTION

She was three and it was the wrong towel.

The bath had been completely fine. The towel wasn't yellow — it was blue — and suddenly she was on the bathroom floor, arching her back, face completely red.

"I hate you, Mommy."

I stood there holding the wrong towel. Heat in my chest. Cycling through every parenting response I'd ever heard. Mostly just frozen.

What I didn't know then: those three words were proof of something I'd spent three years building.

—

Here's the thing no one tells you.

Children say "I hate you" to the people they feel safest with.

Not to teachers. Not to grandparents who give them candy and never make them do hard things. They save those words for the people who have proven, over and over, that the relationship holds anything.

When a child screams "I hate you," she's running an experiment. The question is: *Am I still yours even when I'm like this?*

She needs the answer to be yes.

—

This is what child development research actually shows: kids with secure attachment express intense emotions *more*, not less. The child who goes quiet and easy when she's overwhelmed has often learned to protect the relationship by suppressing.

The one who screams? She's betting you can hold it.

—

You don't need a perfect script in the moment. You need to stay.

"I know you're really upset. I'm right here."

Not a lesson. Not "you don't mean that." Just: I see you. I'm not leaving.

Later, when the storm is over: "You can be angry with me. I'll always be here, even then."

—

I found the yellow towel eventually. Under the sink.

I handed it to her without a word. She stopped crying almost immediately. Held her arms out.

"I love you," she said, into my shoulder, like nothing had happened.

She'd already forgotten. I hadn't.

But what stayed with me wasn't the sting. It was this: *this child knows she can be anything here and still be held.*

That might be everything.

## LINKEDIN POST

There's something counterintuitive I keep coming back to in conversations about child development.

The children who say "I hate you" to their parents — loudly, in the middle of a terrible moment — are often the children with the most secure sense of belonging.

It sounds backwards.

But here's what attachment research shows: a child who knows they unconditionally belong feels safe enough to fall apart. To show their worst. To say the most threatening thing they know. Because somewhere in their nervous system, they've learned: *this relationship can survive me.*

The child who suppresses that anger — who goes quiet, compliant, easy to love — has often learned something different. That the relationship might not hold their rage. So they protect it by performing.

For anyone working with families, or raising children, or thinking about what unconditional belonging actually looks like in practice:

The question underneath "I hate you" is always the same: *Am I still yours, even when I'm like this?*

The job — in that moment — isn't to stop the feeling. It's to become the answer.

—

We write personalized books that give children that message before they're old enough to read. Because what they're told about who they are becomes the voice they carry for the rest of their lives.
