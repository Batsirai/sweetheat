## INSTAGRAM CAPTION

The laminated schedule I made in May is now somewhere in the recycling.

By the end of week two, we had officially moved from "intentional summer activities" to "please just go outside for twenty minutes" to "okay fine, screens, I don't care."

And I've been carrying this low-grade guilt about it. Like I'm behind on the summer I was supposed to give them.

So I want to say this out loud to whoever needs it:

Week three is not when summer falls apart. It's when it actually begins.

The first two weeks, everyone's still performing. The novelty of no schedule is carrying it. Then around day eighteen, the novelty ends — the kids get bored, they start fighting over things they have never once fought about, someone says "I hate summer" — and you think: *I have not done enough.*

But boredom is actually where the good stuff lives.

When your kid has no agenda, no camp, no one scheduling their hours — they have to become interesting to themselves. Find out what they like. Invent something. That's not a crisis. That's development.

And here's what the research on childhood memory consistently shows: kids don't remember the activities. They remember who was there.

Not the museum trips. The Tuesday afternoons when you were just around. When you were doing your own thing and sometimes interruptible. The loose, unhurried rhythm of someone being home.

Your kids need you to be real with them this summer. Not relentless.

So if week three looks like cereal for dinner and an ant documentary and nobody fighting about it — you might be doing exactly right.

You don't have to earn the summer. You just have to be present for the parts you can.

## LINKEDIN POST

Something I keep returning to in the third week of summer:

We've outsourced the meaning of a good childhood summer to activity density.

Camps, enrichment programs, curated experiences — the implicit belief underneath all of it is that a good summer is a full one. That the value is in what happens during it.

What developmental research actually shows is different.

Children don't build their strongest memories around activities. They build them around presence. Around the texture of ordinary time spent near someone who isn't performing for them. Around the feeling of a home that's unhurried and available.

There's also something worth naming about boredom: the child who is never bored never has to discover what they actually find interesting. Never has to invent something. Never has to find out that they are a person with inner resources that exist independent of external stimulation.

The parent who doesn't rush to fill every empty summer hour is giving something that looks like doing nothing from the outside. It isn't.

I think this has parallels in organizational culture. Teams that create space for genuine unstructured thinking — without every hour optimized — tend to surface more original ideas. Leaders who are present without an agenda are often the ones people remember.

The week the schedule falls apart might be the week something more real begins.
