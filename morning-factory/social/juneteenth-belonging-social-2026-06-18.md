## INSTAGRAM CAPTION

June 19, 1865.

The war was over. The proclamation had been signed two and a half years earlier.

But the people in Galveston, Texas didn't know. They had been legally free — and didn't know it yet.

Juneteenth names the gap between being free and *knowing* you're free. The day the news finally arrived.

I've been thinking about that gap.

Because children can be safe. Loved. Growing up in homes where they are wanted. And still carry messages, absorbed from the world around them, that their worth is conditional.

That love is something you earn.
That belonging is something granted by others, not something you were born into.

Those messages come from everywhere — classrooms, comparison, a culture that is very good at teaching people to earn things. And children absorb them even when home is telling a different story.

What parents do, in a thousand ordinary moments, is deliver the news.

When you tell your daughter that her worth isn't in whether they picked her at recess.
When you tell your son that crying doesn't make him less.
When you say — again and again, in words and presence and the quiet texture of home — *you are already loved. Not because you earned it. You just are.*

You're being the one who arrives with the announcement: what you've been living under is not the truth about you.

That's what we want for our children. The news, delivered early. Before the world tries to teach them otherwise.

Happy Juneteenth. Freedom is worth celebrating — in history, and in the ordinary moments where we hand it to our children.
