## INSTAGRAM CAPTION

She told me she hadn't touched the markers.

Her hands were purple.

I almost laughed — and then I didn't, because I realized what was actually happening. She wasn't being defiant. She was scared.

That moment changed how I understand lying in children completely.

Here's the thing: children don't lie because they're bad. They lie because they've done the math — *is the truth safe right now?* — and decided the answer is no.

When a child trusts that the relationship can hold whatever happened — that you might be frustrated, but you're not going anywhere — they tell you things. Real things. Uncomfortable things. The four-year-old who volunteers what she did at preschool before you even hear about it. The seven-year-old who admits the homework didn't get done and tells you why.

The child who confesses (purple hands and all) is showing you something: she thinks the room is safe.

The child who hides isn't broken or devious. She just hasn't found out yet that truth is safe to hand over.

You don't need a better lecture on honesty. You need your face to stay soft in the moment when truth lands badly. You need to let the room stay standing. A thousand times. Over years.

That's what builds an honest child. Not the consequence for lying — the safety around telling the truth.

## LINKEDIN POST

Most organizations treat dishonesty the way most parents do: as a character problem.

But research on child development offers a different frame — one that applies surprisingly well to workplaces.

Children lie when the cost of truth is too high. When they've learned, through accumulated experience, that disappointing the authority figure puts their standing at risk, they stop telling the truth. They manage perception instead.

From the outside, it looks like compliance. From the inside, it's protection.

The same dynamic shows up in teams and organizations. Employees who've been punished for surfacing problems don't stop having problems — they stop reporting them. The silence that follows isn't peace. It's managed perception.

What creates honest children (and honest teams)? Not rules about honesty. Safety around truth-telling. Not just saying "you can always come to me" — proving it, repeatedly, by not making truth costly.

The child who grows up knowing their place isn't contingent on their performance becomes the adult who tells you about the problem before it's a crisis. Not because they were trained in honesty. Because they never learned to hide.
