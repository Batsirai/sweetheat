## LINKEDIN POST

Seven days into summer, my daughter came to me with her hands on her hips.

"He keeps following me around."

Three rooms away: her brother, preparing his defense.

If you work in family content, early childhood, or parenting support — or if you just have kids at home — this pattern is everywhere right now.

Summer sibling fighting peaks in weeks 2-4 for a structural reason.

During the school year, children get their regulatory needs met from multiple sources: movement, peer interaction, challenge and resolution, social feedback. Summer removes all of that abruptly.

A child who's under-stimulated and mildly dysregulated will reach for whatever connection is nearest to discharge the feeling. In most homes, that's a sibling.

What looks like "picking a fight" is actually an attempt at regulation through contact.

What doesn't help: separating and adjudicating. It stops the visible behavior without addressing the underlying need.

What does help: ten minutes of undivided attention, one child at a time. No phone, no agenda. Kids who are regulated through their primary attachment have less need to use a sibling as an outlet.

This is the core of the AlreadyLoved framework: identity and belonging are upstream of behavior. Children who feel their place in the family is secure don't have to fight their way into connection.
