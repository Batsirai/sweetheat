## INSTAGRAM CAPTION

There was purple marker on her hands.

She looked me right in the face and said she hadn't touched them.

My first instinct was to address the lie. Talk about honesty. Make it a moment.

And then I stopped, because I looked at her face — and she wasn't defiant. She was scared.

She lied because the truth felt risky.

Not because she's a bad kid. Not because she's on some path I need to worry about. Because she did the math — the way little kids do without knowing they're doing it — and decided the truth might cost her something she couldn't afford to lose.

Here's what I've been learning about lying in kids:

It's almost never about character. It's almost always about safety.

A child who lies is protecting something — usually belonging. The approval of the people they love most. The image they've built in your eyes. The connection they're terrified of losing.

The question that changed how I think about this isn't "why does my child lie?"

It's "what is the cost of truth in our house?"

Because every time a child tells you something hard and it goes okay — not consequence-free, but okay, connection intact — they recalibrate. The truth gets a little less expensive. Over months and years, this compounds.

And eventually you're not just getting the small truths. You're getting the real ones.

The middle school stuff. The friendships they're not sure about. The things they're ashamed of. The questions they're embarrassed to ask out loud.

You get those conversations because of what happened with the purple markers.

## LINKEDIN POST

There's a parenting reframe that took me a while to internalize, and I keep coming back to it:

When a child lies, the instinct is to address the lie. To teach honesty. To add a consequence so the behavior doesn't repeat.

But the research points somewhere else.

Children lie significantly more when they anticipate harsh or unpredictable reactions to the truth. They lie less — sometimes dramatically less — when they feel confident the truth will be received without shame or disproportionate punishment.

Which means the lie is not really a character issue. It's a safety signal.

A child who lies is calculating, in whatever way a child calculates, whether the truth is survivable. Whether telling you will cost them something they need — approval, closeness, the picture you have of them.

The most effective intervention isn't more emphasis on honesty.

It's lowering the cost of truth.

When parents respond to hard, uncomfortable honesty with curiosity instead of punishment — when they say "I'm really glad you told me that" before they say anything else — children recalibrate. They file that away. They come back with harder truths.

And eventually, years from now, you're the parent who gets the real conversations. Not because you demanded honesty. Because you made it safe.

This principle applies at work too. Teams that feel safe to admit mistakes, surface problems, flag bad news early — they outperform teams where the cost of truth is too high.

We're all doing the same math, in different contexts. The question is the same: what is the cost of truth here?

Written a longer piece on this today for a parenting audience, linked below — but the underlying dynamic is worth thinking about wherever you lead people.
