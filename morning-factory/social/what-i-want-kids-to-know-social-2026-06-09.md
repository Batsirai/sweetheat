## INSTAGRAM CAPTION

There are things I've been trying to get into my kids early.

Before the world has fully gotten in.

Not lessons, exactly. More like... installations. Things I want sitting so deep in them that by the time they need them — the hard years, the lost jobs, the relationships that end, the moments when they can't figure out who they are — they don't have to remember I said it.

They just know it. From the inside.

The one I keep coming back to is this:

You don't have to earn your place here. You already have one.

School is going to teach them otherwise. Social media definitely will. Sports and friendships and a thousand tiny interactions are going to whisper, constantly, that love is a reward and belonging is conditional.

I can't build a wall against all of that.

But I can try to build something older. Something that was true before they ever did anything impressive — back when they were just here, taking up space and needing to be fed.

The world can argue with a lot of things. But it's hard to argue with something that's just always been there.

## LINKEDIN POST

There's a difference between teaching a child what to do and putting something in them about who they are.

Most of childhood is about the former. Schools, sports, extracurriculars — they teach skills, rules, how to perform well and what good performance earns.

What rarely gets explicitly taught: that their worth was there before any of it.

I've been thinking about what the best parents I know are actually doing — and increasingly I think the most important work isn't instruction. It's identity formation.

The parent who builds something in a child that says *you belong here before you've done anything to deserve it* is doing something that all the skills training in the world can't replicate.

Because skills help you in the good years. Identity holds you in the hard ones.

The child who grows up knowing their worth doesn't depend on their performance — not as a thing they were told, but as a thing they've always felt — that child is less vulnerable to the anxiety, perfectionism, and approval-seeking that shows up in so many high-achieving adults.

They already have what they're often unconsciously working for.

That's the investment worth making early. Not certificates. Not activities. The unconditional belonging that makes everything else feel manageable.
