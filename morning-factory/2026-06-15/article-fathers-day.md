# What Dads Actually Want to Give Their Kids (But Don't Know How to Say)

My dad was not a man of many words.

He showed up to every game. He fixed things without being asked. He'd press money into my hand when I left for college like he was trying to hand me something he couldn't name.

I didn't understand for years what he was trying to say. Then one day, when my own kid was small — maybe four, maybe five — she climbed into my lap after a hard day and said, "Daddy, do you love me even when I'm not good?"

And I thought: *Oh. That's what he was trying to say. Every single time.*

---

## The Thing Dads Carry

There's this weight most fathers walk around with and almost never talk about. It's not regret exactly. It's more like: *I love this person more than I know how to say, and I'm terrified they don't know it.*

Not because they've been neglectful. Not because they haven't tried. But because somewhere between the morning chaos and the evening commute and the homework and the bath and the "just five more minutes" — the actual thing, the *I see you and I am so glad you exist* thing — never quite made it out of their chest.

They coach the team. They pack the lunches. They sit in the dark during the scary parts of the movie so their kid won't be alone.

But the words? The ones that would travel from now all the way into adulthood and become the voice inside their child's head when life gets hard?

Those are the hardest ones.

---

## What Kids Are Actually Listening For

Here's what most parents don't realize until it's almost too late: children are always answering a question, even when no one's asked it out loud.

*Am I loved? Am I enough? Is who I am — not what I do, not how I behave, not how I perform — but who I *am* — good enough for this?*

They're listening at bedtime. They're listening after they've messed up. They're listening in the quiet moment when nothing has happened at all.

And they're building their inner voice — the one they'll carry into every hard moment of their lives — out of whatever they hear most often.

Research in developmental psychology has shown for decades that a child's sense of self-worth is constructed in relationship. Not built from achievements. Not accumulated through praise for performance. But absorbed, slowly and steadily, through the felt experience of being *known* and *wanted* exactly as they are.

The children who grow up knowing this — who have it in their bones before the world ever tells them otherwise — are the ones who can weather failure without falling apart. Who can handle a hard season without deciding they are the problem.

Not because they were shielded from difficulty. Because someone told them, early and often, who they already were.

## What Most Father's Day Gets Wrong

We've made Father's Day into a day for dads to receive things — brunches, tools, ties. And there's nothing wrong with any of that.

But here's what I notice: the gifts that mean the most to fathers aren't the ones they get. They're the ones they give.

The phone call from a grown child who says, *You always made me feel like I could do anything.*

The moment a teenager, in a rare unguarded second, says, *I know you're proud of me.*

The quiet certainty a father carries when he watches his kid face something scary and sees them reach for steady ground — because somewhere inside them, there's a foundation that was built word by word, year by year, in the ordinary evenings of childhood.

That's what dads actually want. Not for Father's Day. For always.

## A Story About Simmone's Dad

Simmone was five when her dad started the ritual.

Every night, after the lights went out, he'd sit on the edge of her bed and tell her three things he loved about who she was — not what she'd done, not how she'd behaved, just *who she was*.

"You notice when someone's sad."

"You're brave in a quiet way."

"You make everywhere you go more interesting."

She didn't always respond. Sometimes she was already half-asleep. But her dad kept going, even on the nights she seemed not to hear.

When Simmone was fourteen, she went through a hard year. A friendship that fell apart. A class that felt impossible. A stretch of weeks where she came home quieter than she'd ever been.

Her dad didn't know exactly what to say. So he sat on the edge of her bed and did what he'd always done.

Years later, she told him: "Dad, those nights were the only time I felt like I was okay."

He hadn't fixed anything. He hadn't solved anything. He had just told her, over and over in the dark, who she already was.

## The Gift That Lasts

If you're a dad reading this today: you don't need a different approach to parenting. You don't need to do more. You don't need to have the perfect words ready.

You just need to find a way to say the thing you already know.

That your kid — the one who drives you crazy and makes you prouder than you've ever been — is already enough. Already loved. Already someone whose identity doesn't depend on what they accomplish or how well they perform.

And if the words feel stuck — if you're like my dad, someone who loves hugely and speaks quietly — there are ways to say it even when the words don't come.

Some dads write letters their kids open when they turn eighteen. Some build a ritual, like Simmone's dad. Some find a book that says it for them — a personalized story where their child's name and identity are woven into every page, so they can read it aloud at bedtime and let the words do what they can't quite manage alone.

The method matters less than the message.

What your child needs to know — what they are *hungry* to know, even if they never ask — is that they don't have to earn your love. They were born into it. They'll be held by it when they fail. It isn't going anywhere.

Say that today, if you can. Find a way to say it if you can't.

What you say today becomes their inner voice tomorrow.
