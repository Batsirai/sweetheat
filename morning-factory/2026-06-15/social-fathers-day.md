He shows up to every game. He fixes the bike tire before you notice it's flat. He sits in the back row of the school play with his phone held up, recording the whole thing shaky because his hands are full of feelings he doesn't know how to say out loud.

Most people think quiet dads love less.

They love the same. They just don't have the vocabulary.

And here's what I've been thinking about this Father's Day: maybe the most meaningful gift you can give a dad isn't something for *him* — it's something he gets to *give*.

A way to finally say the thing.

There's a little girl named Simmone whose dad sat on the edge of her bed every single night and told her three things he loved about who she was — not what she did, not how she behaved, just *who she was*.

"You notice when someone's sad."

What you say today becomes their inner voice tomorrow.

This Father's Day, if your words feel stuck — find a way to say it anyway. A ritual, a letter, a book that says it for you every night at bedtime.

Your kid is listening, even when it looks like they aren't.

## LINKEDIN POST

Most of us know at least one dad like this.

He builds things. He fixes things. He shows up without being asked. He loves in a language that doesn't have many words.

But here's what I keep coming back to as a parent and as someone who thinks about how children develop their sense of self:

The kids who grow up knowing they are already enough — before achievement, before performance, before they've proven anything to anyone — aren't the ones who were told the right things at exactly the right moments.

They're the ones who absorbed it slowly, over years, through a thousand ordinary moments where someone looked at them and said, in some way: *I see you. You don't have to earn this.*

Research in developmental psychology has shown this consistently: a child's self-worth isn't built through praise. It's built through felt belonging. Through the experience of being known and wanted exactly as they are — not a better version, not the version that behaves — but the one standing right there.

Father's Day has a tendency to be about what dads receive. But the most meaningful Father's Days I've heard about are the ones where a dad gave something that mattered.

Words. Presence. A ritual. Something that said: *I see you. Not just what you do. You.*

What you say to a child today becomes the voice inside their head when you're not there. The voice that shows up during failure, during loneliness, during the moments when life is hard enough to make them question whether they're fundamentally okay.

Fathers: you don't have to have the perfect words. You just have to find a way to say the thing you already know.

They're listening.
