Simmone was four when she started saying it to herself.

I'd hear her in her room, playing alone, things going sideways with her toys or her blocks or whatever small drama was unfolding in her imagination. And instead of crying for me or melting down, I'd hear this small, certain voice: *"It's okay. I'm good at figuring things out."*

I stood in the hallway the first time and put my hand over my mouth.

Because that was mine. That was something I'd said to her — once? twice? — when she was frustrated and couldn't make something work. I hadn't said it as a technique. I'd said it because I believed it.

And somehow it had moved in. It had become hers.

## What You're Actually Doing When You Talk to Your Kids

Most of us think of the things we say to our children as information transfer. We're explaining the world. Correcting behavior. Expressing love when we think to. Answering the questions that don't stop.

But child development researchers have been saying for decades what parents sometimes feel without being able to name: the sentences we repeat become the sentences our children think in.

Not eventually. Not in some distant formation-of-character way. *Now.* Young children between two and eight are building the inner architecture they'll carry for the rest of their lives — the voice that narrates their experience, the instincts they have about their own worth, the baseline assumption they hold about whether they are safe, loved, and capable.

That architecture is built from language. Specifically, from the language closest to them.

Your voice. The words you repeat. The things you say in ordinary moments — at breakfast, after a hard day at school, in the car, at bedtime when you think they're already asleep.

Those words don't disappear when you stop saying them. They go inside.

## The Difference Between What You Say and What They Hear

Here's where it gets a little tender.

Most of us are saying loving things. We mean well. We're trying.

But there's a gap that's worth understanding — between the words we say and the message our children receive.

*"I'm so proud of you"* — said after a performance, a good grade, a first soccer goal — means something like: *when you do something worthy, I am proud.* The child absorbs not just the pride but the structure of it: *my belonging depends on doing something worth being proud of.*

*"Good job"* — said after they clean up, behave, cooperate — means: *this is what earns approval.* The child learns, quietly, that approval is conditional. Not because we intended that. Because that's what the sentence structure taught.

None of this is accusation. It's just how children work. They're meaning-making machines, and they are very, very good at finding the pattern beneath the words.

The question isn't whether we're saying loving things. Most of us are.

The question is: what structure is the love inside?

## Identity Before Behavior

There's a way of talking to children that lands differently.

Not correction. Not praise for performance. Something earlier in the chain: naming who they *are*, not what they *do*.

*"You noticed she was sad. That's you — you have always been someone who pays attention to people."*

*"That was hard and you stayed with it. That's the kind of person you're becoming."*

*"You don't have to be perfect here. You're already someone I'm happy to know."*

These sentences do something different. They don't create a performance loop — do something, get love, do more, get more love. They offer belonging on the front end. Identity before behavior. Love before earning.

When children hear their identity named — not aspirationally, but as a statement of what already exists — something settles. They stop auditioning. They get quieter inside.

And paradoxically: children who aren't spending their energy proving themselves tend to behave better. Not because they're performing for approval, but because they're secure enough to actually be themselves — and their truest selves are, usually, pretty good.

## The Sentences Worth Repeating

This isn't a list of scripts. You know your child. You know what's true about them.

But if you're looking for somewhere to start, these are the kinds of sentences that tend to move in and stay:

Sentences about belonging: *"You belong in this family. That's not something you can lose."*

Sentences about character: *"That's the kind of person you are — someone who keeps trying."*

Sentences about being seen: *"I noticed what you did. I see you."*

Sentences about the future: *"You're going to be okay. I've watched you. You figure things out."*

Sentences about unconditional presence: *"I'm not going anywhere. Even on the hard days."*

Say them in ordinary moments. Not ceremonies. Not special talks. At breakfast when things are fine. Before bed when things are not. In the car when they're staring out the window.

They won't always respond. They might not even look up. 

They're still listening.

## What Books Have to Do With It

Stories are one of the places identity gets shaped, quietly, without effort.

A child who hears their name in a story — not just as a label but woven into a narrative about who they are, what they carry, where they belong — receives something different than information. They receive a reflection.

*Here is a world where someone with your name is brave. Gentle. Already loved. Already whole.*

That reflection doesn't replace your voice. Nothing does. But it joins it. It becomes part of the chorus.

Simmone still says *"I'm good at figuring things out"* to herself. She's seven now. She says it in her sleep sometimes, I think.

I didn't teach her to say it. I just said it once or twice, in a tired, ordinary moment, because it was true.

And it moved in.
