## INSTAGRAM CAPTION

When your child is at their worst — cup on the floor, voice raised, everyone exhausted — they're actually asking their most important question.

*Am I still okay here? Do you still love me?*

Not consciously. Not on purpose. But every act of defiance, every meltdown, every push-away is a child probing the edges of belonging to see if it holds.

And what we say in those moments — in those tired, frayed, I-have-nothing-left moments — goes somewhere. It gets filed. It becomes the voice they hear in their own head for the rest of their lives.

That's not a pressure. It's an invitation.

You don't have to be eloquent. You don't have to have the perfect speech. You just have to say the thing that's already true:

*Nothing you do changes this. You are already loved.*

Say it when it's easy. Say it when it's hard. Let them see your face when you say it.

Because what you say today becomes their inner voice tomorrow.

## LINKEDIN POST

There's a reframe in child psychology that I think belongs in every leadership conversation about culture, too.

When a young child acts out — defies, pushes away, melts down — most adults try to address the behavior. Fix the symptom. Apply the consequence.

But researchers studying child identity formation for the past six decades have found something more fundamental underneath: the behavior is a question.

*Am I still safe here? Am I still valued? Do I still belong?*

The child isn't defiant. They're testing attachment. They're checking whether the belonging is conditional.

Now think about the adults on your team doing quiet versions of the same thing. The person who goes quiet when criticized. The one who over-delivers because they're not sure they're enough. The one who leaves before they can be let go.

The research on what resolves this — for children and, remarkably, for adults — is the same: consistent, explicit communication of unconditional belonging. Not just *felt*, but said.

"You are still part of this. That doesn't change."

Behavior follows identity. And identity, for most people, is still being built by the voices of the people they trust most.

You are one of those voices. In whatever room you're in.
