## LINKEDIN POST

Mother's Day is 17 days away, and I keep thinking about this piece I read about how children build their internal self-concept.

The short version: by age 7, a child's internal narrative — the voice that tells them whether they are capable, lovable, worthy of belonging — is assembled largely from the words of the adults who loved them first.

Not the love those adults felt. The love they said out loud.

I think about this in the context of every organization I've ever seen try to build culture. Leadership teams pour enormous energy into *feeling* invested in their people. Into carrying genuine care for the team.

And then those people still leave. Or they stay and disengage. Or they deliver exactly to spec and never bring the real thing.

Because unexpressed care doesn't build psychological safety. Said care does.

"You belong here."
"What you bring to this is irreplaceable."
"That was hard and you stayed in it. That's who you are."

The research on what creates confident, secure, highly functional children is almost embarrassingly transferable to what creates the same in adults.

Say it. Specifically. Often. In the hard moments, not just the performance-review ones.

This piece is written for mothers. But the principle runs everywhere: identity precedes behavior, and identity is built out loud.
