## INSTAGRAM CAPTION

---

Yesterday was Mother's Day.

Today is quieter.

And I keep thinking about a moment from last week — my youngest did something small. Finished her plate. And without thinking I said, "Good job, honey."

And she looked up at me with this little glow. Like she'd passed a test.

Which made me realize: she thinks there's a test.

I never meant to create a test. I never sat down and said *love arrives after the good thing*. But somehow, in the daily rhythm of response and praise and "good job" and "I'm so proud of you when..." — she learned.

She learned that warmth comes in response to behavior.

And I don't want that for her.

I want her to know what I knew intellectually but hadn't yet handed her in a form she could hold:

**You were enough before you did anything.**

Not almost. Not "you're getting there." Not "keep going and you'll be worth something."

Already. You already belong here. You already matter. This house, this family, this love — it isn't contingent on your performance. It was here before you could talk. It'll be here when you mess up. It's not something you earn with good days. It's just... yours.

That's the thing I keep coming back to.

What I say today becomes her inner voice tomorrow.

In twenty years, when things are hard — when she's in some room where she doesn't know if she belongs — what will she hear?

I want it to be this.

## LINKEDIN POST

---

I've been thinking about a pattern in parenting that mirrors something I see in workplace culture.

We praise performance. We reward output. We celebrate the metric.

And slowly — quietly — people learn that their worth is contingent on what they produce.

In children, researchers call this "contingent self-worth." It's self-esteem that lives on a performance axis: up when things go well, down when they don't. And the data is clear — children with contingent self-worth show higher rates of anxiety, perfectionism, and fear of failure as they grow.

Not because their parents didn't love them.

Because the love, as expressed, felt conditional.

The alternative is what child development researchers call "unconditional positive regard" — a concept from Carl Rogers that sounds soft but is actually the most structurally sound thing you can build a human on.

It means the child knows: my belonging here is not up for negotiation. It doesn't rise and fall with my behavior. It is simply given.

Kids who grow up with this don't become entitled. The research doesn't show that. They become brave. Because when you can't lose belonging, you stop spending your energy protecting it.

You can actually grow.

The phrase that keeps coming back to me: *what you say today becomes their inner voice tomorrow.*

In twenty years, your child will face a hard room. A hard moment. A season when nothing seems to be working. And they will hear a voice inside them.

That voice is being formed right now.

I wrote about this for AlreadyLoved — what identity-first parenting actually looks like in practice, with specific phrases and the research behind them.

Worth sharing with any parent who's ever felt like they're accidentally getting this wrong.
