# The Night I Realized I'd Been Teaching My Kid to Earn Love

It was 9pm. The house was finally quiet.

My daughter — four years old, wild hair, very strong opinions about socks — had been in bed for thirty minutes. I was sitting at the kitchen table with a cup of tea I'd reheated three times, finally actually drinking it.

And I was thinking about something she'd said at dinner.

She'd eaten almost all of her pasta. She looked up at me while I was clearing plates and asked, very seriously: *"Mama, are you proud of me?"*

I said yes, obviously. Of course I was proud of her. She'd eaten her pasta.

But something about the way she asked it stayed with me. She wasn't asking in the delighted way kids ask when they've just done something spectacular. She was asking more like... checking. Like she needed to run the numbers and see if they added up.

*Am I okay? Did I do enough today?*

And I realized, sitting there with my tea finally warm, that I was the one who'd taught her to ask that question.

---

## I Didn't Mean To

It happened so slowly I didn't notice.

Every time she ate well, I praised the eating. Every time she shared, I praised the sharing. Every time she sat quietly, every time she listened, every time she got through a hard moment without a meltdown — I told her she was so good, I was so proud, she did such a great job.

All of it true. All of it loving. All of it, over time, quietly teaching her: *Love is a response to performance.*

Not on purpose. Not cruelly. Just... incrementally.

Because that's how most of us were raised. Because praise feels like love and it is love. Because we don't have a better script handed to us.

But I sat there at that kitchen table and thought: she's four. And she's already checking to see if she measured up.

---

## The Question Behind the Question

When a four-year-old asks "are you proud of me?", they're not really asking about dinner.

They're asking: *Am I okay? Am I enough? Am I still loved even if tomorrow goes worse?*

Most children ask some version of this. It's healthy to want to know. What varies is what they expect the answer to be — and whether they've already learned that the answer might change depending on what they do.

A child who has been loved lavishly but always in response to behavior will ask that question and brace a little for the answer.

A child who has been told, clearly and often, *you are loved before you do anything right* — that child still wants to know. But they already have a sense of the answer.

---

## What I Started Doing Differently

I didn't stop praising her. That's not the point.

I just started separating the two things that had gotten tangled up: praise for behavior, and declarations of identity.

Praise for behavior: "You ate your pasta tonight and that makes me happy."

Declaration of identity: "And none of that changes how much I love you. You could've thrown the pasta across the room and I'd still love you exactly this much. I don't want you to forget that."

You say both. But you make sure the second one doesn't depend on the first.

At bedtime, I started saying things like:

*"You had a hard day and an easy day and a normal day — and I love you through all of them the same."*

*"You don't have to be good tomorrow to deserve love. You already have it."*

*"You're my person. That's not something you can lose."*

She still asks "are you proud of me?" sometimes. But she asks it differently now. More like curiosity than a calculation. More like she already knows the answer and wants to hear it anyway.

---

## The Sentence I Wish I'd Started Saying Earlier

If I could go back to the beginning — to the first year, the newborn months, before habits were formed — I would have added one sentence to every bedtime.

Not instead of the praise. Alongside it.

*"You are already loved. Not because of today. Just because you're you."*

Short. Simple. But said night after night, it becomes the voice a child hears when no one is watching. When something goes wrong. When the world starts asking them to earn their worth.

---

## This Isn't a Guilt Post

I want to say this clearly: if you're reading this and feeling like you got it wrong, that's not what this is.

You didn't get it wrong. You loved your child with what you had.

Most of us were raised by people who also loved us with what they had. And most of them were doing the same thing we were — connecting love to performance because that's the language they were given.

Noticing it is enough. You can start tonight.

One sentence. Any sentence that says: *you are already loved, before you do anything to prove it.*

Tonight, before the lights go out.

That's where it starts.
