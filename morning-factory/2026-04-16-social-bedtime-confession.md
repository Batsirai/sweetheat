## Instagram Caption

There's a night I keep thinking about.

My son was five, and he'd had one of those days. You know the days. The ones where everything goes wrong before 9am and just stays that way.

That night at bedtime, I started my usual thing — the little list of everything I loved about him from the day.

And I had nothing.

Everything I usually said was tied to what he *did*. You were so brave. I was so proud of you. You were such a good helper. And that day — none of it fit.

So I stumbled. I said something like, "Even on the hard days, I love you."

And I watched something flicker across his face. Not relief. More like a question.

*Even on the hard days?*

That flicker stayed with me for weeks.

Because here's what I've come to understand: when a child's bedtime affirmations are tied to what they did that day, they quietly learn that love notices performance. We don't mean to teach that. But they're listening beneath the words.

The shift isn't complicated. Instead of telling them what they *did*, we tell them who they *are.*

Not "you were so brave today." But: *"You are brave. Even when everything feels too big, brave is already in you."*

Not "I'm so proud of you." But: *"You are so loved. Not because of today. Just because you're you."*

It sounds small. Read it again slowly and notice what's different.

The first column attaches your warmth to the day's events. The second makes it permanent. Untouchable. Not subject to revision in the morning.

That's what we're building, one bedtime at a time. Not perfect days. A foundation.

## LinkedIn Post

I run a brand for parents called AlreadyLoved Kids. Our whole thesis is one sentence:

*When kids don't know they're already loved, the world teaches them to earn love.*

I used to think that was something that happened to other families. Then I listened to myself at bedtime one night and realized I was doing it too.

Every night I recapped the day. "You were so brave." "I'm so proud of how kind you were." "You were such a good helper." My son would glow.

Then he had a genuinely terrible day. And I had nothing for the list.

That's when I saw it: every phrase I used was attached to performance. I wasn't naming who he was. I was narrating what he did.

It's a subtle difference with a significant long-term effect. Child development research on self-concept and attachment shows that children as young as three begin forming what researchers call *conditional self-esteem* — a sense of worth that depends on external validation. Most of us are feeding that system without knowing it, with the most loving intentions.

The reframe is simple but requires deliberate practice:

- Instead of "You were so brave today" → "You are brave. Even when things are hard."
- Instead of "I'm so proud of you" → "You are loved. Not because of today — just because you're you."
- Instead of "You were such a good boy" → "Nothing you do will ever make me love you more or less. You are already loved."

The first column ties warmth to the day. The second makes it permanent.

What we say becomes their inner voice. I think about that a lot — not with pressure, but with intention.
