# What I Wasn't Saying to My Daughter (And Why It Mattered More Than I Knew)

I told her I loved her every single day.

I'm not saying that as a defense. I'm saying it because I think a lot of us are in the same place — saying *I love you* a hundred times, meaning it every time, and still somehow leaving out the most important part.

I didn't figure this out from a parenting book. I figured it out from watching her.

She was seven. She'd gotten a part in the school play — a small one, but she wanted it so badly. And when she found out, she ran to me with her whole face lit up. The first thing she said wasn't *I got the part.* It was:

*"Are you proud of me?"*

I said yes, of course, immediately. And she glowed.

But I drove home that evening with something sitting in my chest that I couldn't quite name. Something about how *happy* she was to make me proud. How she'd run to me first, not to share the joy — but to *earn the response.*

That's when I started to wonder what I'd been accidentally teaching her.

---

## The Love We Show Versus the Love They Feel

Here is what I had been doing:

I praised her when she did well. I comforted her when she was sad. I celebrated her wins and kissed her hurts. I was *present.* I was *loving.*

But almost all of my warmth — the warmth she could most easily feel — was *responsive.* She did something, I responded. She succeeded, I celebrated. She struggled, I showed up.

She was learning, without anyone saying a word, that love is something you receive. That it flows toward you after you do something worthy of it.

She was learning to earn me.

And she was so good at it. She worked hard to make me proud. She was careful not to disappoint me. She brought me her wins and hid her failures because the wins got the better response.

None of this was her fault. None of it was mine. But it was happening, quietly, in the space between what I said and what she heard.

---

## What Was Missing

I had been saying *I love you.*

What I hadn't been saying was *you are already loved.* 

Those are not the same sentence.

*I love you* is a statement about my feelings.

*You are already loved* is a statement about her nature.

One says: I feel this toward you.

The other says: You carry this with you. You don't have to earn it. You don't have to keep it. It doesn't depend on what happened today.

Children — especially young children — don't automatically connect *I love you* to *I am loved no matter what.* That connection has to be explicitly built. Named. Said out loud, in ways that leave no room for a conditional.

The research on this is clear and kind of heartbreaking: kids as young as three are already constructing beliefs about whether love is contingent. By the time they're seven or eight, those beliefs are calcifying into patterns — how they relate to teachers, to friends, to effort, to failure.

What we say in the early years doesn't just shape behavior. It becomes their inner voice.

---

## The Shift That Changed Things

I didn't overhaul anything. I just started saying one more thing.

Not when she succeeded. Not when she was struggling. Just — in ordinary moments. When she was eating breakfast. When I was tucking her in. When we were in the car and the radio was off.

I started naming her.

Not her achievements. *Her.*

*"You know what I love about you? The way you notice when someone's left out."*

*"You've always been like this — you think about things longer than most people. That's a real thing about you."*

*"I want you to know something. I love you because you're you. Not because of how today went."*

Small things. They felt almost too small at first. But she started doing something different.

She started talking to me about the times she *didn't* do well.

She'd come home and tell me she'd been unkind to a friend, or she'd failed a spelling test, or she'd gotten scared and said nothing when she should have said something. She started trusting me with the parts that weren't worth celebrating.

That's when I knew something had shifted. She wasn't performing for me anymore. She was *talking* to me.

---

## What Kids Need to Hear That We Forget to Say

Here is what I've come to believe:

Children are listening for a specific message, and most of us deliver it on accident — if we deliver it at all.

The message is: *You are already worthy of love. Not because of what you did today. Not because of the grade or the goal or the good behavior. Because you are you, and that is enough.*

It sounds simple. But for most kids, it is genuinely not the message the world sends them. School measures them. Sports rank them. Social dynamics tell them they're only as good as their last moment of coolness.

At home, we get to be the counter-voice.

Not in a loud, therapeutic, effortful way. Just in the ordinary moments. The ones that add up.

*Say what they need to hear.* Not just what's true — all of it is true — but what they *need.* What they're quietly waiting for someone to say first, before they've done anything to deserve it.

Because what you say today becomes their inner voice tomorrow. The voice that speaks up in the college dorm room at midnight. The voice in the car on the way to the interview. The voice in the first week of a new relationship, when they're deciding whether or not they deserve to be there.

That voice is being written right now.
