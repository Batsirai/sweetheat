## Instagram Caption

She ran to me with her whole face lit up.

She'd gotten the part in the school play — a small part, but she'd wanted it so badly. And the first thing she said wasn't "I got it." It was:

*"Are you proud of me?"*

I said yes, immediately. She glowed.

But I drove home that evening with something sitting in my chest I couldn't name. Something about how *happy* she was to make me proud. How she'd come to me not to share joy — but to earn a response.

I had been saying "I love you" every single day. I meant it every time.

But I hadn't been saying the other thing.

*"You are already loved."*

Those aren't the same sentence.

"I love you" is about my feelings. "You are already loved" is about who she is.

One says: I feel this.
The other says: You carry this with you. You don't have to earn it. It doesn't depend on today.

The shift was small. I just started naming her — not her achievements, but *her* — in ordinary moments. Breakfast. The car. After bedtime, when the room was quiet.

*"You know what I love about you? The way you notice when someone's left out."*
*"You've always been like this — you think about things longer than most people."*
*"I love you because you're you. Not because of how today went."*

She started talking to me about the times she *didn't* do well.

That's when I knew something had changed.

What you say today becomes their inner voice tomorrow. You're writing it right now, in the ordinary moments.

Make sure it says: you are already enough.

## LinkedIn Post

There's a quiet difference between two things most parents say interchangeably.

**"I love you."**
**"You are already loved."**

One is a statement about how you feel. The other is a statement about who your child is.

I spent years on the first and forgot the second — until I noticed my daughter running to me not to share joy, but to earn my response. She'd gotten a part in a school play. Her first words weren't "I got it." They were: *"Are you proud of me?"*

She had learned, without anyone teaching her explicitly, that warmth flows toward you after achievement. That love is something you receive when you do the right things.

Most children learn this in well-functioning, loving families. Because most parental warmth is responsive — we celebrate wins, comfort failures, show up for the moments that need us. That's not wrong. It's love. But when it's the *dominant pattern*, children start to map it: do something, earn something.

The shift isn't complicated. It's naming a child — not their achievements, but *them* — in ordinary, unprompted moments.

*"You've always been like this — you notice when people are left out."*
*"I love you because you're you. Not because of how today went."*
*"You don't have to earn this."*

Within weeks my daughter started telling me about the times things went wrong. She stopped hiding failure. She started trusting me with the parts of herself she wasn't proud of.

That's the fruit of "you are already loved." Not compliance. Not performance. Connection.

The world will teach your children that love must be earned. Home can teach them the opposite — and that lesson, said early and often, becomes the voice they carry forever.
