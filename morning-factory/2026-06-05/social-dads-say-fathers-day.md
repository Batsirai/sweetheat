She's nine now. And she still says it to herself.

When she was four, she fell off her bike. Not badly — more startled than hurt. And instead of "you're okay, get up," my husband knelt down and said: *"You're someone who gets back up."*

I watched it happen. Watched her face change.

Five years later, she says it in the mirror before hard days. She says it when something at school isn't going well. She says it like she owns it, like it was always true.

Because it was. He just told her first.

There's something specific about a father's voice. Researchers talk about how dads name their children's character in ways that shape self-concept in social situations, under pressure, in the moments when no one else is watching.

But you don't need the research to know it. You've felt it.

This Father's Day, the most powerful gift a dad can give isn't a lesson or a speech. It's a sentence. Said with presence. About who that kid already is.

*"You notice when people need something."*
*"You have a strong sense of what's fair."*
*"Nothing you do changes what you are to me."*

Say the thing. Even if it feels small. Even if you think they already know.

They need to hear it. In your voice. With your name on it.

Happy Father's Day to all the dads and father figures quietly building the inner voice of the next generation. 💛

## LinkedIn Post

A father knelt down next to his four-year-old daughter after she fell off her bike. 

Instead of "shake it off" or "you're fine," he said: *"You're someone who gets back up."*

She's nine. She still says it to herself.

I think about this a lot when it comes to leadership, to parenting, to how we name people in the moments that matter.

There's extensive research on father-child attachment showing that how a father *names his child's character* — not praises their performance, but names who they are — shapes how that child navigates social pressure, professional adversity, and relationships decades later.

The mechanism is elegant: children don't yet have a stable answer to "who am I?" They're borrowing the draft from the people who love them first. The dad who says "you're someone who keeps going" is writing part of that draft.

Three patterns show up consistently in the research on identity-forming father-child communication:

**Seeing words** — naming what you've noticed, not what you hope for. "You have a tender heart. I see it."

**Naming words** — claiming a character quality for the child before they can claim it themselves. "You don't quit easy. I've watched you."

**Belonging words** — anchoring the child's worth outside of performance. "Nothing you do changes what you are to me."

This isn't soft psychology. It's structural. Identity stability — knowing who you are before external feedback tells you — is one of the strongest predictors of long-term resilience, leadership capacity, and emotional regulation we have.

The children who are easiest to manipulate are the ones who don't have a stable answer to "who am I?" They'll take the answer from whoever offers it most confidently.

The children who don't need constant external validation? They already have an answer. Someone gave it to them, in a quiet moment, before they could fully process what it meant.

This Father's Day: give your kids the answer. Not "you're amazing." Something specific. Something true. Something that doesn't need to be earned.

That sentence is the gift.
