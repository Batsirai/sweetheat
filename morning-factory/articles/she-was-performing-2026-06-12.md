# She Was Performing. I Didn't Know I'd Taught Her To.

*by AlreadyLoved*

---

**Meta description:** Your child might be performing for your love without either of you knowing it. Here's how to recognize it — and how to stop it.

**Target keywords:** child performing for approval, conditional love parenting, raising confident children, children seeking approval, identity first parenting, unconditional love child

---

She came down the stairs on a Sunday morning with her hair done.

She is four.

She doesn't do her own hair. I hadn't said anything about going anywhere. We had no plans. She had brushed her hair and come down with this careful little smile — and my first reaction, God help me, was to say *oh, don't you look pretty today.*

She lit up.

And something in me went quiet. Because I recognized what I'd just done. I'd handed her a reward for looking a certain way, and she'd been waiting for it.

---

## The moment I'd been missing

I didn't notice it at first. Children who perform for approval are easy to miss — especially when you love them. They seem fine. They seem happy, even. They run toward you with drawings. They show you their somersaults. They ask, over and over, some version of the same question: *do you see me? Am I enough?*

What I'd been calling enthusiasm, I was starting to wonder about.

She wanted to please me. That's not a bad thing, at four, or at any age. But there's a difference between a child who delights in sharing something with you and a child who is performing to stay safe.

Safe. That's the word. I kept coming back to it.

When a child isn't sure their belonging is guaranteed, they start managing your perception of them. Not consciously — they're four, not calculating. But instinctively, the way a body turns toward warmth. If love is something you receive for being good, or pretty, or funny, or smart, then the smart move is to be those things as loudly as possible.

She was being smart.

---

## What the research says (and what I wish I'd known earlier)

Researchers who study child development have a term for it: *conditional positive regard*. It's the experience a child has when they sense that your warmth, your approval, your delight in them — is conditional. Not on love, exactly. You never said you didn't love them. But on performance.

The kid who cleans their room and gets a hug. The kid who gets a good grade and watches your face relax. The kid who makes you laugh at dinner and gets that warm version of you for the rest of the night.

These are not terrible moments. Every one of them has happened in my house.

But if *most* of a child's positive feedback is tied to what they did rather than who they are, they start to learn a lesson you never intended to teach: that they are something to earn.

Dr. Wendy Grolnick and others studying self-determination theory have found that children raised with high conditional positive regard tend to show more contingent self-worth as they grow — meaning their sense of okay-ness rises and falls with performance. They also tend to show more anxiety, more suppression of negative feelings, and in some cases, more disconnection from their own intrinsic motivation. They stop doing things because they love them. They do things because they're watching your face.

My daughter was watching my face.

---

## What it looks like in the real day

I started paying attention in a different way. Not anxious — I don't want to be that parent who pathologizes everything. But paying attention.

She hesitates before she shares her drawings now. She holds them for a beat, watching me, before she hands them over.

She asks "did I do good?" about things that shouldn't require evaluation. A somersault. A snack she made herself. The tower she built and knocked over.

When I'm on my phone and she shows me something, she'll repeat it — louder, more exaggerated — until I put the phone down. Not out of entitlement. Out of uncertainty. She needs to see my face to know she's okay.

She catches her own mistakes before I say anything. Not problem-solving — *apologizing*. Preemptively. At four.

None of these things, on their own, means anything. Children are naturally oriented toward their parents; that's good attachment, not performance. But the texture of it — the monitoring, the seeking, the hesitation — that was familiar to me. Because I recognized it from somewhere.

I recognized it from myself.

---

## Where she learned it

This is the part that's hard to write.

She didn't learn it from a stranger. She didn't learn it from school or TV or a friend's house. She learned it from watching me receive her.

The way I lit up when she got dressed well.
The way my voice warmed when she shared her food.
The way I praised her for being helpful, being kind, being good — always for the doing, rarely for the being.

I never said "I love you when you behave." But I showed her something close: *I am most happy with you when you are performing well.*

That's not a character flaw. It's just how most of us were wired. We grew up in systems that rewarded performance — school, family, faith communities, friendships. We learned that love has a grammar: it comes after you've earned the right sentence. So naturally, we pass that grammar to our kids without meaning to.

The things we've absorbed most deeply are the things we teach most fluently.

---

## The sentence she needs to hear first

I've been reading about identity-first parenting lately, and the thing that won't leave me is this: *identity precedes behavior*. A child who knows who they are doesn't need to perform to establish it. They already have it. It was given to them before they did anything to deserve it.

What my daughter needs — before the praise, before the feedback, before any evaluation of what she did — is to know that I see *her*. Not the drawing. Not the hair. Not the somersault. Her.

The thing I've started doing — quietly, without making it a project — is naming what I see before it becomes something evaluable. Not "good job with your hair" but "I love waking up and seeing you." Not "that drawing is so pretty" but "I love that you always make time to create things."

It's a small shift, and I won't get it right every time. But it's the shift from evaluating her output to simply witnessing her presence.

She is not a performance. She is a person. She existed before she did anything to impress me, and she will be loved long after the somersaults and the hair and the careful little smiles have stopped.

I want her to know that so deeply that it becomes the ground under everything else.

That no matter what she does, or doesn't do, or does wrong, or stops trying, or fails at, or gets praised for — none of it changes the fact that she is already loved. Not because she earned it. Not because she looks a certain way on a Sunday morning.

She's loved because she's here. That's the whole thing.

---

## What to do with the performing child

Here's what I've found — and I am not an expert, just a mom watching her kid closely — that helps:

**Name her without the doing.** "I love you" without any qualifier. "I'm so glad you're mine" for no reason. Say it when she hasn't done anything. Especially then.

**Receive the drawing before you evaluate it.** Before "oh, I love the colors!" try "tell me about this." Let her be the authority on her own creation. Your job is to be interested, not to grade.

**Let the mistakes land softly.** When she spills something, notice how you respond. Not because she'll remember the spilled milk — but because she'll remember whether your love got smaller when things went wrong.

**Watch how you praise.** Praise is good. But praise attached only to performance teaches performance. Try praising her character, not just her output. Not "good job cleaning your room" but "you're someone who takes care of things."

**Say the thing out loud.** Not just "I love you." Say: *"Nothing you do could make me love you less. Nothing you do could make me love you more. You're already the whole thing."*

She might not understand every word. But something in her will hear it. And what she hears now will become the inner voice she carries when she's 17, or 34, or sitting up in the middle of the night wondering if she's enough.

You are writing that voice right now, in the way you receive her on a Sunday morning.

Write it well.
