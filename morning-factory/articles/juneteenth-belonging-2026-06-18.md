June 19, 1865.

The Civil War was over. The Emancipation Proclamation had been signed two and a half years earlier. The law had changed. But in Galveston, Texas, the news hadn't arrived.

The people there had been legally free — and didn't know it.

Juneteenth is named for the day Union soldiers arrived to announce what had already been true: that the war was over, that the proclamation held, that they were free.

Not the day freedom was decided. The day they found out.

---

## The Gap

I've been thinking about that gap.

The gap between *being* free and *knowing* you are free.

For the people in Galveston, that gap was two and a half years. Two and a half years of living under conditions that the law no longer allowed. Two and a half years of not knowing that something had already changed.

That gap — between what is true about you and what you've been told is true about you — is one of the most powerful ideas I know. And as a parent, it hits somewhere close.

Because children can be safe. Loved. Growing up in homes where they are wanted and cared for. And still carry messages, absorbed from the world around them, that their worth is conditional.

That they have to earn their place.

That love is something you get when you perform well enough.

That belonging is something granted by others, not something you were born into.

Those messages come from everywhere: from classrooms, from comparison, from the way adults talk about success and failure, from a culture that is very good at teaching people to earn things.

And children absorb them even when the home is telling a different story.

---

## What Parents Can Do

Juneteenth is a celebration of the news arriving. Of the gap closing. Of someone showing up to say: *what you've been living under is not the truth about you.*

That's also what parents do, in a thousand ordinary moments.

When your child comes home from a hard day feeling like they're not enough — and you sit with them instead of rushing past it.

When you tell your daughter that her worth isn't in whether they picked her at recess.

When you tell your son that crying doesn't make him less.

When you say, again and again, in words and presence and the ordinary texture of home: *you are already loved. Not because you earned it. You just are.*

You're delivering a message the world may not. You're being the soldier who arrives with the announcement: what you've been living under is not the final word.

---

## How to Talk About This With Your Kids

For young children, Juneteenth can be introduced simply:

*"A long, long time ago in our country, some people weren't treated as free even though they were. Today we celebrate the day they found out they were free — really free. We remember it because freedom is something worth celebrating. And we remember the people who waited so long to find out."*

For older children, you can go deeper — into the history of enslavement, the Emancipation Proclamation, the news that didn't travel fast enough, and what it meant when it finally did.

And there's a conversation worth having that goes alongside the history:

*"One of the things we want for you is to know — before the world tries to tell you otherwise — that you already belong. You don't have to earn your place. You have one. You always did."*

That's the Juneteenth gift for your children: the news, delivered early.

## Freedom and Belonging Are the Same Story

When people are free, they can be themselves. They can live according to who they actually are rather than who they've been forced to perform.

Belonging is the same. A child who knows they are already loved doesn't have to spend their energy managing your perception of them. Doesn't have to perform for their place in the family. Can be themselves — the full, complicated, sometimes-difficult version — because their belonging isn't at stake.

That freedom is what AlreadyLoved is for. To say the thing before the world says otherwise. To write the child's name into a story of identity and love so that it goes into them early, before they've been taught to earn it.

Juneteenth is a day to celebrate freedom. It's also a good day to ask: what does my child know about their own?
