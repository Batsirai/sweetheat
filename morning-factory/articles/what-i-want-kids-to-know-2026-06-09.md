# What I Want My Kids to Know When I'm Gone

**Meta description:** Not a list of advice — a list of what I want already living in them before they need it. The things I hope feel like instinct by then.

**Target keywords:** what to tell your children, raising children intentionally, words of wisdom for children, what parents want their kids to know, raising children with identity, intentional parenting legacy

**Content pillar:** identity | **Format:** blog | **Purpose:** brand_building

---

Not when I'm going soon. I'm planning to be around a long time. I have every intention of embarrassing them at prom and crying too loud at graduations and becoming a grandmother who has too many opinions about how often I get to see the grandchildren.

But there are things I've been trying to get into them early. Before the world has fully gotten in. Things I want sitting at the bottom of them so deep they feel like instinct rather than memory — the way you know how to swim even years after you last swam.

I think about this more than I talk about it. Because when I'm honest, I'm not worried about whether they'll be successful. I'm worried about whether they'll know certain things when success doesn't come, or when it comes and it isn't enough, or when life turns out different from what they expected and they need something to hold onto.

Here is what I want them to already have by then.

---

## That they don't have to earn their place here

The world is going to spend decades trying to teach them otherwise. That love is a reward. That belonging is contingent. That you are the sum of your achievements, your usefulness, your performance.

School teaches it. Sports teach it. Social media will teach it loudly and constantly.

I am trying to build something in them that predates all of that. Something older. That they arrived in this world already worthy. That their value was not assigned by their behavior or their grades or how many people liked them or whether they did the impressive thing.

They already have a place here. It was theirs before they did anything.

I want them to know that down to the marrow — not as a thing I once told them, but as a thing they have never not known.

---

## That they are allowed to be wrong

There is a particular kind of person who cannot admit they are wrong. They are hard to be close to. Something in them — some fear, some thing that was installed early — makes being wrong feel like an annihilation.

I don't want that for them.

I want them to know that being wrong is just a moment in a life, not a verdict on a person. I want them to know that the apology is available — that repair is possible — that they can break something and fix it and still be whole.

One of the things I keep trying to show them without making a speech about it is that I am wrong sometimes. I get frustrated when I shouldn't. I misread a situation. I say something that lands wrong. And then I come back and say: I got that wrong. I'm sorry.

Not because I'm performing humility. Because I want them to have a picture of what that looks like — a person they love, admitting an error and surviving it. So that when they are grown and wrong about something important, they already know: this is survivable. This is what we do. You say it and you come back.

---

## That the hard things are survivable

They're going to lose things. People, maybe. Relationships they thought were permanent. Dreams that felt certain.

I can't protect them from any of it. I wish I could more than I'm able to say.

But I can try to give them a body that knows, at some level, that it has been through hard things before and come through. Not unscathed. Not unchanged. But come through.

This is part of why I don't try to prevent every hard feeling. Not because I want them to suffer — I would take every heartache from them if it were mine to carry — but because I want them to know their own resilience from the inside. Not as a thing I told them they had. As a thing they have actually felt.

The six-year-old who cries and then stops crying and goes back outside. The eight-year-old who has a bad week and makes it to the end of the week. The teenager who loses a friendship and eventually, slowly, makes a new one.

Each time: *I have been through something hard and I am still here.* 

I want that to be something they know in their bodies, not just their heads.

---

## That they don't have to be what anyone needs them to be

There is a particular kind of love that is actually a demand dressed up as affection. It says: I love you, and I need you to be a certain way, and when you are that way I will give you more of me.

I have been loved that way. Most of us have.

I don't want to give it and I don't want them to need it.

I want them to know — specifically, concretely — that their job is not to manage my feelings. It is not to perform happiness for me when they are sad. It is not to pretend things are fine when they are not, in order to protect me from the discomfort of their truth.

Their job is to be themselves — whoever that turns out to be, in whatever direction that grows — and to know that I am interested in meeting that person, whatever they become.

This one I have to work at. Because I have preferences about who they'll be. I notice when they do the things I hoped they'd do. I have to keep choosing, over and over, to be curious rather than prescriptive. To ask questions rather than steer. To let them reveal themselves to me rather than trying to shape the revelation.

But I want them to know: they are not here to become something for me. They are here to become themselves.

---

## That the people who love them are proud of things they haven't noticed yet

The quiet things. The way she always notices when someone is left out. The way he stops what he's doing when someone needs something, even if it inconveniences him. The gentleness with the small things — the bugs they move off the sidewalk, the dogs they ask to pet, the younger kids they are patient with.

Those things are visible to me. I'm watching.

I want them to know I'm watching not the things they're trying to be noticed for — the grade, the goal, the performance — but the things they don't even know they're doing. The ones that are already just who they are.

Those are the things I'm proudest of. And I hope, somewhere, they feel that landing. Even on the days when they feel like they haven't done anything worth noticing.

---

## That they are already loved

This is the one I've been circling around. The thing I most want in them when I'm not there to say it.

You don't have to earn it. You already have it.

Before the grade. Before the game. Before they've figured out who they are or what they're for or whether they're doing it right.

The world is going to spend a long time teaching them that love has to be deserved. I'm trying to give them something older than that argument. Something that's been true since before they could do anything impressive — since they were just here, in the world, taking up space and needing to be held.

That's when it started. And nothing since then has changed it.

I want that to be the thing at the bottom of them. The thing they reach for when the world has its hands on them, when success didn't come, when the relationship ended, when they can't figure out what they're doing or who they are.

The knowledge — not the hope, the knowledge — that somewhere, someone already loves them and always did. Before any of it. Independent of all of it.

I am not always going to be here. The people who tell them the true things won't always be in the room.

So I'm trying to put it inside them, deep enough that it goes with them wherever they go.

You are already loved. Full stop. Before the rest of the story.
