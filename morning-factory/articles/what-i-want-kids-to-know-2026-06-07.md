# What I Want My Kids to Know When I'm Gone

**Target keywords:** what to tell your children, raising children intentionally, words of wisdom for children, letter to my children, what parents want their kids to know
**Meta description:** Not a letter for the end — a list for right now. The things I keep trying to get into them before the world gets there first.
**Word count:** ~1,600
**Format:** Blog
**Seed date:** June 6, 2026

---

Not when I'm gone soon. I'm planning to be around a long time.

But I've been thinking about what I want inside them before the world fully gets in. Things I want sitting at the bottom of them so deep they feel like instinct rather than memory. Not rules. Not advice. Something older than that.

Here's what I keep trying to get in.

---

## That they were loved before they did anything to earn it

This is the one I come back to most.

The world is going to spend decades teaching them the opposite. That love is a reward. That belonging is earned. That the way to matter is to perform well, achieve something, become someone impressive.

They'll feel this in small ways before they even have words for it. In the look on someone's face when they succeed versus when they fail. In how a room feels when they're the best at something versus when they're not. In which version of themselves gets more warmth.

I want something in them that is older than all of that. Something that got there first.

I want them to know: before you were impressive, before you were capable, before you'd done a single thing I could point to — you were already mine. You already mattered. You were already loved.

Not loved *because* of anything. Just loved.

If they carry that, they'll have something to come back to when the world tries to tell them otherwise. And it will try.

---

## That they don't have to perform for the people who love them

There's a kind of exhausting thing that happens to people who grew up believing love was conditional. They become very good at reading the room. Very good at becoming whoever the room needs them to be. Very good at managing other people's feelings about them.

And they're often wonderful people. Successful. Warm. Good at relationships in a surface way.

But somewhere underneath there's this engine running — *am I still okay, am I still okay, am I still okay* — and it doesn't stop.

I don't want that for my kids.

I want them to know they don't have to perform for me. They can come to me falling apart. They can come with the ugly feelings and the bad days and the version of themselves they're not proud of. I'm not going anywhere.

That's not the same as saying I'll approve of everything they do. I won't. There will be consequences and conversations and moments where I'm disappointed and I say so.

But disappointed is not the same as gone. Upset is not the same as out.

*You can still reach me from inside the mess.*

---

## That feelings are not the same as facts

Especially the feeling of *I am not enough.*

Everyone feels it. It arrives so naturally, so early, and it sounds so much like the truth. The feeling of not being enough is one of the loudest sounds in a person's interior life.

I want them to know it's a feeling. It's not information. It's not evidence. It's a response — often to comparison, or to a difficult moment, or to a nervous system that hasn't had enough sleep.

They don't have to argue with it. They don't have to fix it. They just need to know it's not the whole story. That *I feel like I'm not enough* is very different from *I'm not enough.*

The feeling will visit. It doesn't get to move in.

---

## That their body is not a project

Somewhere between ten and twelve, children start receiving a very clear cultural message that their body is something to be optimized. Something to look at critically. Something to manage.

I want to get there before that message does.

Your body is the house you live in. It gets tired and it gets hungry and sometimes it hurts and sometimes it does extraordinary things. It is yours. It is not for evaluation, not for other people's comfort, not a measure of your worth.

The world is going to suggest otherwise. Loudly, consistently, in a hundred subtle ways.

I want something in them that knows better than that before the suggestion arrives.

---

## That hard things don't mean you're doing it wrong

When I was growing up, hard things felt like evidence. Like: *this is hard, which means something is wrong with me, because it shouldn't be this hard for other people.*

I know better now. But I had to learn it the slow way.

I want them to know early: hard doesn't mean wrong. Hard sometimes means important. Hard sometimes means you're growing into something.

The relationship that takes work isn't necessarily broken. The skill that takes practice doesn't mean you lack talent. The period of life that is exhausting and uncertain doesn't mean you've gone the wrong direction.

Sometimes hard just means *this is hard right now.*

You're still okay. Keep going.

---

## That it's okay to not know who they are yet

Identity takes time. Longer than it looks from the outside.

Some people know early — they know what they love, what they're for, where they belong. Most people don't. Most people spend their twenties and even their thirties in a kind of becoming. Trying things, losing things, finding out what fits and what doesn't.

This is not a problem. This is how it's supposed to go.

I want them to have permission to not have it figured out yet. To be in progress. To try something and love it for a while and then outgrow it. To be a different version of themselves at 16 than they were at 12, and different again at 22.

They are not behind. They are becoming.

---

## That I will still be here when they think they're too much

There will be moments when they're convinced they're too much. Too emotional. Too messy. Too needy. Too loud. Too broken. Too hard to love.

I want them to know: that's the exact moment to come find me.

Not after they've cleaned themselves up. Not once they've got it together and have something coherent to say. *Right then. In the middle of the too much.*

Because that's when the message matters most: you are not too much for me. You were never too much for me.

I have room for all of it.

---

## That they were always enough

Not enough-plus-effort. Not enough-once-they-figure-it-out.

Enough. Now. As they are.

It's probably the thing I'll spend the rest of my parenting life trying to say in every possible language. In the way I respond when they fail. In the way I hug them when they're ugly-crying. In the thing I say when I tuck them in, when I drop them off, when I see them across a room being exactly themselves.

*You are already loved.*

Before the grade, before the game, before you've become whatever you're going to become.

You were already mine.

---

I'm writing this down because the days are long and also fast, and I know I'll forget to say it in the moment. I'll be tired. There will be dinner on the stove and someone will be asking about something else.

But I want it said. Somehow, in some form, early and often.

This is what I want in them when I'm gone.

The knowledge that they were already loved before they did anything to earn it.

That's the whole thing. Every other lesson is downstream of that one.
