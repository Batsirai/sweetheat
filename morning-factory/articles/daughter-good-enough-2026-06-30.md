# The Night I Heard My Daughter Tell Her Stuffed Animal She Was "Good Enough"

It was 11:07pm.

I was walking past her room to get a glass of water. She was four years old and she'd been in bed for two hours — or supposed to have been. The hall light was off. The house was quiet.

And then I heard it.

She was talking to her bunny. That worn-out, one-eyed rabbit she'd had since she was seven months old. And she was saying, in this very small, very serious voice:

*"It's okay. You're good enough. Even if you mess up."*

I stood in the hallway and could not move.

---

I've thought about that night hundreds of times since.

Because she was four. She didn't learn "good enough" from a podcast or a self-help book. She wasn't quoting something she'd read.

She was rehearsing something she'd heard. Testing out a phrase that had landed somewhere in her, seeing how it felt in her own mouth.

I believe she was practicing being kind to herself the same way she'd heard us be kind to her.

The question that's stayed with me is: what if she'd been rehearsing something different?

## The Voice They're Building Right Now

There's a researcher named Lev Vygotsky who studied how children learn to think. One of the things he found was that the inner voice — the one we all have that narrates our lives, coaches us through hard things, talks us down when we spiral — starts as an outer voice.

It starts as the voice of someone talking to the child.

By around age three, children begin doing something called "private speech" — talking out loud to themselves while they play, while they work through problems, while they process emotions. They're not talking to anyone. They're practicing having a voice inside.

And the content of that voice? It comes from the voices around them.

Not perfectly. Not literally. But the material is borrowed.

If a child hears "you're not listening" and "why can't you just behave" and "you're making this so hard" — that's the material they're working with. It doesn't mean they become broken. Kids are resilient. But it does mean the inner voice they build has those phrases in it somewhere.

And if they hear "you are loved, even when it's hard" and "you're trying so hard, I can see that" and "nothing changes how much I love you" — that's in there too.

## What We Don't Realize We're Doing

I want to say something gently here, because I've lived on the wrong side of this.

We don't usually fail our kids through big dramatic moments. We fail them through small ones. The distracted yes-yes-yes while we're on the phone. The "just stop crying" when we have nothing left. The corrections stacked up at bedtime when we meant to say something warm.

None of that is evil. All of that is being human.

But children don't sort our tired moments from our true ones. They take it all in.

This isn't meant to make you panic. I promise. Because the same thing that's true about the hard moments is true about the good ones: those land too.

The "I love you for exactly who you are" at breakfast. The "I noticed how kind you were today" in the car. The "you belong here, always" at bedtime. That material goes in.

Children are writing their inner voice right now. We're handing them the words.

## What My Daughter Taught Me at 11pm

When I heard her that night, my first instinct was to sneak away without saying anything. Let the moment be. Don't interrupt it.

And then I thought — what if I went in and confirmed it?

So I pushed her door open, very softly. She looked up at me, not startled, just surprised.

"Mom? I couldn't sleep."

"I know, sweet one. I heard you talking to Bunny."

She looked down at him, a little shy. "I was telling him it was okay."

"That he was good enough?"

She nodded.

"He is," I said. "And so are you. Even on your hardest days."

She didn't say anything. She just held the bunny a little tighter. And then:

"Even when I cried at dinner?"

"Even when you cried at dinner."

She was quiet. Then she settled back against her pillow.

I kissed her forehead and walked back to the hall. And I stood there in the dark for a minute, thinking about what it means to be the voice inside your child's head.

## What Identity-First Parenting Actually Looks Like

There's a lot of talk about raising confident kids. Strong-willed kids. Resilient kids.

Most of it focuses on behavior. What to do when they throw a fit. How to respond when they won't eat. Techniques for tantrums, strategies for routines.

And some of that is useful. But it misses the root.

Behavior follows identity. Always.

A child who knows they are already loved — before they perform, before they behave, before they make good choices — that child has something to draw from when things are hard. An interior resource. A settled sense of who they are.

A child who has to earn love — who learned somewhere, even accidentally, that your warmth depends on their behavior — that child is always managing. Always tracking. Never quite sure they're safe.

We don't usually teach that explicitly. We teach it through the small moments we don't even notice.

Which means we can also unteach it. Through the small moments we choose deliberately.

## The Simplest Practice I Know

I'm not going to give you a program. A step-by-step system with a printable. That's not what this is.

What I'll tell you is the simplest thing I've found that works.

Before your child falls asleep — even if it's been a hard day, especially if it's been a hard day — say something about who they are. Not what they did. Not what you need them to do tomorrow. Who they are.

"You are my favorite person to wake up next to."

"I love you not because you were good today, but because you are mine."

"There is nothing you could do that would make me stop loving you."

It doesn't have to be long. It doesn't have to be perfect. It just has to be true.

Say it enough nights in a row, and one night, you'll walk past their room at 11pm, and you'll hear them say it to their bunny.

And you'll know it landed.
