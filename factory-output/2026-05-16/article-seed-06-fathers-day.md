# A Letter to Every Dad Who Wonders If He's Doing It Right

This isn't for the dads who have it figured out.

This is for the one who drove home from work last night still thinking about the meeting. Who came through the door too late for bath time. Who heard about his kid's day in a summary from someone else.

This is for the dad who coaches the soccer team but isn't sure his kid knows why he does it.

For the one who grew up in a house where things were provided but certain things were never said.

For the one who tries — God knows he tries — and still isn't sure it's enough.

If that's you: this is for you.

---

## What Kids Are Actually Looking For

Here's something that might surprise you.

Research on what children say they need most from their fathers isn't complicated. It's not the activities. Not the vacations. Not the college fund.

It's presence. And specifically, it's the words that come with presence.

Children who hear their fathers name their identity — who hear *I'm proud of who you are, not just what you do* — carry that differently than children who only hear praise for achievement. The difference shows up in how they handle failure. How they talk to themselves when things go wrong. How they define their own worth.

The world spends a lot of time trying to teach kids what they're worth through performance. Schools do it. Sports do it. Social media, eventually, will do it in ways we can't fully predict yet.

A father's voice is one of the few things that can cut through all of that. That can say: *you are already enough. You don't have to earn it here.*

That's not a small thing.

---

## What Might Have Been Missing

A lot of the dads I know didn't grow up hearing it either.

They grew up in homes where love was demonstrated through provision. Through showing up. Through doing. Their fathers were good men — hardworking, present in their own way — but certain things were never spoken.

And those unsaid things live somewhere.

They live in the quiet uncertainty that follows you. The voice that wonders: *am I doing enough? Am I enough?*

If your own dad never said the words — if you grew up inferring love from actions but not quite sure you were beloved — you know exactly what I'm talking about.

You also know that you want something different for your kids.

The ache you carry is actually a compass. It's pointing you toward the thing your child needs to hear from you.

---

## What They Need to Hear

It doesn't have to be a speech. It doesn't have to be perfect.

In fact, the most powerful thing you can say is something simple, in your own voice, on an ordinary Tuesday.

*"I want you to know something. You don't have to do anything to earn my love. You already have it. You've always had it."*

Or at bedtime, hand on the shoulder:

*"You are my favorite. Not because of what you did today — just because you're you."*

Or after the game, win or lose:

*"I'm not proud of you because of the score. I'm proud of you because of who you are."*

Kids need to hear these things before they're in crisis. They need to hear them on normal days so that when the hard days come — and they will — there's already something solid inside them to stand on.

You can build that. Not with grand gestures. With repetition. With small, ordinary moments of naming them.

---

## The Fear Underneath

A lot of dads don't say these things not because they don't feel them — but because they're afraid of what it means if they do.

Afraid it sounds weak. Soft. Sentimental.

Afraid their kid will think they're easy — that love without conditions is love without standards.

Here's what I'd offer: those fears are about you, not about your child.

Your child doesn't need a father who withholds love to build resilience. The research doesn't support that. What builds resilience is a secure attachment — the deep knowledge that love is stable and available, not something that disappears when you fail.

Children who know they are unconditionally loved don't become soft. They become brave. They try things and fail and get back up because they aren't afraid of losing your love in the process.

Saying *I love you no matter what* doesn't lower the standard. It removes the threat. And kids freed from the threat of losing love are actually more able to hear your guidance, your expectations, your honest feedback.

The words aren't weakness. They're the foundation everything else stands on.

---

## If You Didn't Hear It, You Can Still Say It

Maybe the hardest thing about being a father is passing on something you didn't receive.

You can't give what you never got — except that you can. People do it every day. It just takes intention.

You don't have to have grown up in a home full of these words to be the father who says them. You just have to say them. Imperfectly. Consistently. Even when it feels strange in your mouth.

*"I love you."*

*"I'm proud of you."*

*"You are already everything I hoped for."*

Say them enough and they stop feeling strange. Say them enough and your child stops needing to wonder.

---

## A Simple Practice for This Father's Day

If you're looking for something meaningful to do this Father's Day — not a brunch, not a round of golf, though those things are fine — here's something quieter:

Find a moment alone with each of your kids. Not a big moment. Not a prepared speech.

Just say the thing you've always felt but maybe never quite said out loud.

Tell them who they are. Not what they do. Not what you hope they'll become. Who they already are, to you, right now.

That conversation — that five-minute, stumbling, earnest, maybe-a-little-awkward conversation — is something they will carry.

For years.

Longer than any gift.
