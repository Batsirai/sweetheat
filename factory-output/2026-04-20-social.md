## LINKEDIN POST

I'm a parent who thinks a lot about what we tell our children.

Not just the explicit things — the "I love you's" and the "good job's." The subtext. The narrative texture. The story forming around them about who they are.

Here's what I've come to believe after years of thinking about this and building around it:

**Identity forms before behavior. Not after.**

We spend enormous energy trying to shape children's behavior. But a child who knows — genuinely knows, in their body and not just their head — that they are kind, brave, curious, enough? They don't need much instructing. The behavior follows.

The hard part: identity doesn't come from lectures. It comes from stories.

Stories told about them. Around them. In front of them. Stories where their name appears and the world seems to have been built with them in mind.

My two-and-a-half-year-old's response when he heard his name in a story for the first time:

*"That's about me."*

Not a question. A recognition.

We built AlreadyLoved around that moment. Around the belief that a child who hears their name woven into a story of identity — who feels known before they're old enough to defend themselves against the opposite message — carries something into the world that lasts.

The world will eventually teach them that love is something to earn. We want them to have a counter-story already running.

*"What you say today becomes their inner voice tomorrow."*

We take that seriously.
