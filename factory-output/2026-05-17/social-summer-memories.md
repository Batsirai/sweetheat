## Instagram Caption

This is the summer planning nobody talks about.

We've got the trips and the camps and the Popsicle budget figured out. The sunscreen is already in the car. The swim lessons are booked.

But here's what I keep coming back to, every summer, before it starts:

*What am I going to say to them?*

Not at the big moments. Not at the fireworks or the beach or the birthday party that finally comes together. 

At the small ones.

The car ride home when they're sandy and half-asleep. The morning when nothing's planned and the day is just... open. The 9pm when they won't stop talking about something that happened three weeks ago and you're tired but they're still going.

Because here's what I know now:

Kids don't remember trips. They remember sentences.

They build their picture of themselves — who they are, whether they're brave, whether they belong — from the words that drift into them when their guard is down. When they're not trying to earn anything. When it's just a Tuesday.

So this summer, before the list gets any longer, I'm adding five sentences to it:

"I was watching you."
"That sounded hard. I'm glad you told me."
"You're the only you."
"I like being your mom."
"That was brave."

That's the summer plan that doesn't expire when August does.

## LinkedIn Post

Something I've been thinking about as Memorial Day approaches:

We're all planning summer right now — trips, camps, activities, logistics.

But the research on childhood memory is humbling. Children's brains aren't archiving itineraries. They're tracking belonging signals. Their nervous systems are always quietly asking: *Am I okay? Am I wanted here? Do I belong?*

Big experiences give them images. Small moments give them answers.

The mom who turned around in the front seat and called her half-asleep daughter brave, not knowing she was listening? That sentence is still playing. Decades later.

In parenting, the return on identity investment is staggering — and the entry cost is almost nothing. A sentence. A pause. A moment of real attention in the middle of an ordinary drive home.

What children carry into their adult lives — their inner voice, the one that tells them they're capable or not, brave or not, worthy or not — is largely assembled in the small unguarded moments. Not the highlight reel.

This summer, we're writing about what's actually worth saying to your kids — not to fill up their schedule, but to fill up their sense of self.
