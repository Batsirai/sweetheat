## INSTAGRAM CAPTION

---

She told me this story once, and I still think about it.

Her son was four. Her husband was running late — coat on, keys in hand. He stopped in the doorway, crouched down to where their son was playing on the floor, and said, quietly, quickly:

*"Hey. I see you. You're a good one."*

Then he was gone.

She watched her son look at the door for a second. Then he went back to his blocks.

Three years later, after his first baseball game — the one where he struck out — her son sat in the backseat of the car, very quiet. And then, almost under his breath, she heard him say to himself:

*I'm a good one.*

---

That sentence had traveled. From a hallway on a Tuesday morning, into a seven-year-old's heart, out his own mouth on the day he needed it most.

This is what the research calls identity language. Not praise. Not feedback. *Identity.* "You're the kind of person who..." "I love the way your mind works." "You're a good one."

Those words don't go into the same place as "nice work today." They go somewhere deeper. They become self-concept. They become the voice a child speaks in when things get hard.

And the part that matters most: you don't need a perfect parenting moment to say them. You need a doorway. Fifteen seconds. Before you leave.

The architecture is already being built — in the ordinary moments, the car rides, the bedtimes, the half-asleep goodnights. The only question is what shape you want it to take.

You don't have to do more. You just have to be intentional with what you're already doing.

Say something true about who your child already is. It will travel further than you know. 🌿

## LINKEDIN POST

---

My friend Daniela's husband said four words to their son before leaving for work one Tuesday morning.

He didn't know it would matter. He was just a dad running late, crouched down in a doorway, fifteen seconds before he left.

**"I see you. You're a good one."**

Three years later, their son — now seven — had a hard game. Struck out. Got in the car quiet.

And Daniela heard him say under his breath, to no one, in the backseat:

*I'm a good one.*

---

I've been thinking about this story in the context of leadership and communication.

Because the mechanism at work here isn't just parenting. It's the difference between **feedback** and **identity language**.

Feedback: "Great job today."
Identity language: "You're the kind of person who doesn't give up."

Research on child development shows that identity statements are stored differently than behavioral praise. They get absorbed into self-concept — the story a person tells about who they are. And those stories travel. They're still active decades later.

The same dynamic plays out in organizations. Managers who tell their people *who they are* — not just how they performed — create teams that carry that identity into hard moments. "You're the person I call when the situation gets complicated." "You see things the rest of us miss." Those statements build something that an annual performance review cannot.

Fifteen seconds in a doorway.

For kids. For teams. For anyone who needs to hear something true about themselves before they walk into a hard day.
