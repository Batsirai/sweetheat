# What My Mom Said About Me When She Didn't Know I Was Listening

**Target keywords:** personalized children's books, identity-based parenting, what to say to your child, building child confidence
**Meta description:** I was nine years old, sitting on the stairs. My mom was on the phone. She didn't know I was there. And what she said about me changed everything. Here's what it taught me about how kids are actually built.
**Format:** blog | ~1,800 words
**Seed:** Engagement — Mother's Day adjacent, evergreen

---

I was nine years old.

It was a Tuesday afternoon. I was sitting on the top step of our stairs, right where the carpet met the hallway floor, in the particular boneless way that children sit when no one is watching them. My mom was on the phone in the kitchen. I hadn't meant to eavesdrop. I'd just stopped to tie my shoe and then I couldn't quite make myself move.

She was talking to my aunt. I could hear the rhythm of a regular catch-up call — the kind where you ask about the kids and then you actually talk about the kids. And then I heard her say my name.

She was describing me.

She said I was the kind of person who always noticed when someone felt left out. She said I'd been that way since I was small — that I'd come home from birthday parties not excited about the cake but sad for the kid who hadn't been picked. She said she thought I was going to be someone who took care of people. She said it like it was something she'd watched grow in me over years. Like it was just true. Like it was just who I was.

I sat there on that step for a long time after she hung up.

I wasn't thinking about any of that consciously. I was nine. I didn't have words for what I was doing. But something was happening — I was filing that away. I was adding it to a folder I didn't know I was keeping.

I still have it. I've carried it for more than thirty years.

## The thing about children and eavesdropping

Here's what I didn't know then, and what I've learned since: what my mother said to me was powerful. But what she said *about* me, when she didn't know I was listening? That was something else entirely.

Children are extraordinarily good at detecting performance. They know the difference between being told something and overhearing something. When a parent looks a child in the eye and says *you're so kind*, the child receives it — and also wonders, just slightly, if you're saying it to make them feel good.

But when a child overhears a parent say *she's always been the one who notices people* to someone who doesn't need to hear it, who has no reason to hear it — there is no filter available. There is no performance to detect. The thing just lands.

It lands in a different place.

I'm not saying we should stop telling our children who they are directly. We should. Say it every day. Say it at bedtime and at breakfast and when you're walking from the car to the door and the moment doesn't have any weight at all. Those words matter.

But the words said *around* children — the descriptions we offer when we think they're not listening, the way we talk about them to grandparents and sisters and neighbors — those words do something the direct ones can't. They offer a kind of proof. They say: this is how you appear to people who have nothing to gain from saying so.

Your child is listening. They almost always are.

## The stories that stick

There's a concept in developmental psychology called narrative identity — the idea that we understand ourselves primarily through story. Not facts. Not characteristics listed in a column. Story.

Who am I? The person who was like this as a child. The person who responded this way when something hard happened. The person people describe with these words when they think I can't hear them.

Children begin forming narrative identity very early — earlier than most of us expect. By three or four, kids are already building a working model of who they are. And they're building it not from instruction but from collection: gathering evidence from the world around them about what kind of person they seem to be.

The evidence doesn't come from what we tell them to do. It doesn't come from correction, or rules, or discipline. It comes from the texture of their daily life — what gets noticed, what gets named, what gets repeated, what gets reflected back to them.

A child who hears "you're always so helpful" once? Maybe nothing. A child who *feels* herself called helpful — in the way the family story gets told, in the way her kindness gets noticed and remarked upon and treated as part of who she *is* — that child starts to experience helpfulness as identity, not instruction. She doesn't need to be taught to be helpful. She is the kind of person who is.

This is why the stories we tell matter. Not the bedtime stories — though those too. The stories we tell about our children. Around them. About them when they're gone. About who they're becoming.

Those are the building materials.

## What she gave me that she didn't know she was giving

My mom was not a perfect mother. She would be the first one to say that, probably in great detail. She worried too much and occasionally said the worrying out loud. She had her own stuff she was carrying. We had hard years.

But she gave me something on that Tuesday afternoon that I have used every decade since.

She gave me a story about myself that had nothing to do with achievement. It wasn't about what I'd done. It wasn't about a grade or a win or a performance. It was about something she'd watched form inside me over years — something she'd noticed, tended to, found worthy of telling.

It said: this is who you are. Not because of what you do. Not because of what you earn. Not because you got it right.

Just: this is who you are.

I've gone back to that step many times. Not literally. But in my mind, in the way adults return to the things that made them. When I've been in situations that called on me to be the person who notices, I've known I could. Because someone said I already was. She just didn't know I heard her.

## The question underneath all of it

Every child is asking something. They ask it in a thousand different ways — with their behavior, with their tantrums, with their bid for your attention in the middle of a conversation you needed five more minutes on.

The question underneath all of it is the same.

*Who am I?*

Not: am I good or bad. Not: did I get it right. But the real one: *Who am I? What kind of person am I? Is there a story about me that's worth knowing?*

We answer that question with everything — with what we celebrate, what we correct, what we laugh at, what we repeat at dinner. And we answer it, maybe most powerfully, with what we say about them when they're on the stairs and we don't know they're there.

This is not a guilt invitation. Please, if nothing else, hear that.

You are already answering. The question of how we shape our children's identity is not a question of whether — it's already happening, it happens in ordinary moments, it happens all the time. You don't need to perform it. You need to see what's already true about your child and let yourself say it. Out loud. Often. Where it might be overheard.

## What this looks like in practice

You don't have to manufacture moments. You just have to notice what you already see.

Maybe it's the way your son asks "is everyone coming?" every time your family plans something — checking that the circle is complete. That's something worth naming: *he's always looked out for the people around him.*

Maybe it's the way your daughter slows down for the hard parts of books — the sad ones, the ones with loss. That's worth noticing: *she feels things deeply. She doesn't look away.*

Maybe it's something you've seen for years, something you've almost started taking for granted: the curiosity, the gentleness, the stubbornness-that-is-actually-persistence, the noise, the warmth.

Say it. Say it to your sister on the phone. Say it to your partner at the end of the night. Say it in the car on the way home from school, directed toward no one in particular.

Your child might be on the stairs.

---

## The words that stay

What you say today becomes their inner voice tomorrow.

That's not a tagline. That's just what we know about how identity forms — that it's built from collected evidence, gathered over years, stored somewhere below conscious thought, retrieved in the moments that ask the most.

The mom on the phone, describing a child she was proud of? She didn't know she was making something permanent. She was just telling the truth.

The permanence came later. On a Tuesday. On a staircase. To a nine-year-old who didn't know she was filing it away.

If your child has a story about themselves — one that says they are already enough, already known, already named — that story will be available to them for the rest of their lives.

You get to put it there.
