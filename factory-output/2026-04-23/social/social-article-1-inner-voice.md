## Instagram Caption

There's a sentence my mom said when I was about seven.

I don't remember the exact moment. I just remember how it felt — like being *seen*, not just noticed.

She said it over and over, in different forms. After hard days. After small wins. Before school. Before bed.

And I still hear it. Right now, at 35, I still hear her voice when I'm in a meeting and something doesn't go right. Not a memory — more like a reflex.

Research has a name for this. It's called the internalized parent voice. And the studies are clear: it's not the big speeches that form it. It's the small, repeated sentences. The ordinary ones.

Which means the things you say in the car, at dinner, right before sleep — those are the sentences your child will carry.

Not what you said at the piano recital. What you said *every Tuesday*.

I started asking myself: what do I want them to hear at 35?

That question changed how I talk to my kids.

Not perfectly. I still rush. I still miss things. But I know the direction.

Say what's true. Say their name. Say it again.

## LinkedIn Post

I've been thinking about something a Harvard study confirmed a few years ago — and I can't get it out of my head as a parent.

The study found that it's not the big moments in childhood that shape a child's sense of self. It's the small, repeated ones.

Not the speeches. The sentences said on a Tuesday at dinner.

Which means most of us are forming our children's inner voice right now — in the ordinary moments we barely notice.

I asked myself recently: what do I want my children's inner voice to say when they're 35 and something goes wrong?

Not what I want them to do. Not what lesson I want them to learn. What voice do I want them to have access to when I'm not in the room?

That question reframed a lot of my parenting.

It's not about being perfect. It's about being intentional about which sentences you repeat.

At AlreadyLoved, this is the whole idea. Not what your child should become. What your child already is — named, known, and told again in a story they love.

If you're in the business of building people (which, as parents, we all are), this is the kind of investment that compounds.
