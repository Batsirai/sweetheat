I was sitting in a meeting last Tuesday when something didn't go the way I'd planned. Not a disaster. Just a small failure — the kind you shrug off, in theory.

But the voice that came first wasn't mine.

It was my mom's. From sometime around 1997, when I was seven and had just done something hard, and she said, *"That's who you are. That's exactly who you are."*

I didn't shrug it off. I got curious. And then I kept going.

She said it so many times that I can't remember the specific moments anymore. Just the feeling of being *seen* by her words, not just noticed.

That voice is still in my head at 35. And I think about that a lot now that I have kids of my own.

## Your Words Don't End When Bedtime Does

There's a concept in psychology called the *internalized parent voice* — and if you've never heard of it, you've still felt it.

It's the voice that shows up when you make a mistake and hears, *"You always do this."*  
Or the one that gets quiet and patient when things fall apart and says, *"This is hard, but you can do this."*

Psychologists who study attachment and identity formation have found that the repetitive phrases our caregivers use in childhood — not the big speeches, not the dramatic moments, but the ordinary things said over and over — are the ones that form our baseline inner narrative.

Not what we heard once. What we heard *again and again*.

## The Sentence That Stays

Think back for a moment.

What's the sentence from your childhood that still lives in you?

Maybe it's something warm. *"You light up a room."* *"I always know I can count on you."*  
Maybe it's something that stings, even now. A throwaway comment that the person who said it probably doesn't even remember.

The sentences that stayed weren't necessarily the loud ones. They were the *frequent* ones. The quiet ones. The ones said at the kitchen table, in the car, right before sleep.

We don't get to choose which sentences our children will carry. But we get to choose which ones we repeat.

## What Research Actually Says About This

A 2019 study from Harvard's Center on the Developing Child found that "serve-and-return" interactions — the small, responsive moments between parent and child — are more predictive of a child's emotional regulation and sense of self than major formative events.

Put simply: the small, repeated moments matter more than we think.

A child who hears *"you always mess up"* ten thousand times builds a different inner voice than a child who hears *"you're someone who keeps trying"* ten thousand times.

Neither is a speech. Neither is a moment. Both are a pattern.

And patterns, unlike moments, stay.

## What I Say Now

I started paying attention to what I was repeating.

Not the big moments. The small ones.

What do I say when my daughter falls? What do I say when my son does something unkind and then immediately feels bad about it? What do I say when something is hard and they want to give up?

I started asking: *what do I want them to hear in thirty years?*

Not what I want them to do. Not what lesson I want to teach. What voice do I want them to have when I'm not in the room?

That question changed how I talk to them.

Not in a scripted, perfect-parent way. I still say the wrong thing. I still rush. I still miss moments I wish I'd caught.

But I have a direction now. And the direction is: *say what you want them to say to themselves.*

## A Simple Ritual

Before my kids go to sleep, I say their names. Their actual names.

And then I say something true about them. Something I noticed that day. Something small.

*"Simmone, I saw you wait for your brother today. That's who you are."*  
*"Leo, you didn't give up on that drawing. I love that about you."*  
*"Emma, you asked if she was okay. That's exactly who you are."*

It takes thirty seconds. I'm not always eloquent. Sometimes I'm tired and I keep it really simple.

But I figure: this is the sentence that might still be in their head at 35. Better make it a true one.

## You Are Already Building an Inner Voice

Here's the thing no parenting article wants to say plainly:

You're already doing this. Every day.

The question isn't whether you're shaping your child's inner voice — you are. The question is whether you're doing it intentionally.

The good news is you don't need to be perfect at it. You just need to be *consistent* about the true things.

What's true about your child? Not what you hope they'll become. What are they, right now, already?

Say that. Repeat it. Let it move in.
