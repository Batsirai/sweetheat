## INSTAGRAM CAPTION

I was nine years old.

Sitting on the top step. Shoe half-tied. My mom was on the phone in the kitchen. She didn't know I was there.

And she said my name.

She was describing me to my aunt — just a regular Tuesday afternoon phone call. And what she said was this: that I'd always been the one who noticed when someone felt left out. That I'd come home from birthday parties not talking about the cake but sad for the kid who hadn't been picked. That she thought I was going to be someone who takes care of people.

She said it like it was just true. Like she'd watched it grow in me for years.

I sat there long after she hung up.

I'm forty-one now. I still carry that Tuesday. I've never once doubted that part of myself — the part she named on that staircase, to someone who didn't need to hear it, for no reason except that it was true.

Here's what I've learned since: children are remarkable at detecting performance. When you look a kid in the eyes and say *you're so kind*, they receive it — and they wonder, just a little, if you're saying it to make them feel good.

But what they overhear? When you describe them to your sister on the phone, when you tell their grandparent what you've noticed growing in them, when you say it out loud to no one in particular because it's just true?

There's no filter available. It just lands.

Your child is listening. They almost always are.

So today — maybe this week — find a true thing about your child. Something you've watched grow in them. Something you love about who they're becoming.

Say it where they might be on the stairs.

## LINKEDIN POST

I've been thinking about something my mom did — not on purpose, but maybe in the most important way.

I was nine. Sitting on the stairs after school. She was on the phone with my aunt, doing the kind of catch-up call where you ask about the kids and then you actually talk about the kids.

She said my name. And then she described me.

Not to me. About me. To someone who had no reason to hear it.

She said I'd always been the one who noticed when someone felt left out. That I came home from birthday parties sad for the kid who hadn't been picked. That she thought I was going to be someone who takes care of people.

She said it like it was just true. Like she'd been watching it grow for years.

I'm forty-one. I've never doubted that part of myself.

---

Here's what that Tuesday taught me about identity formation — and why it matters for how we raise kids and, honestly, how we lead teams.

Children detect performance. They know the difference between being told something and overhearing something. When we look them in the eye and say *you're so brave*, they receive it — and they wonder if we're saying it to make them feel better.

But what they overhear? When the words are offered to someone who has nothing to gain from hearing them? There's no filter available. It just lands.

The same is true for the people we lead. The descriptions we offer in the room when someone isn't there — the way we characterize someone's strengths to a client, the way we talk about a team member's growth in a meeting they're not in — those descriptions travel. They shape how people see themselves.

What you say about people, around them and away from them, matters as much as what you say to them.

For my son (and everyone I work with): I'm trying to be more like my mom on that Tuesday. To say the true things, out loud, where they might be overheard.

What you say about someone becomes part of the story they carry.
