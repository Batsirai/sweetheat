## LinkedIn Post

Pinterest's 2026 Parenting Trend Report landed this month with a striking headline: "no phone summer" searches are up 340% year over year.

I've been sitting with that number.

Parents everywhere are quietly deciding to put the phone down more this summer. And every piece of content responding to this trend is instructional: *how to do* a screen-free summer. Activities. Rules. Family contracts. Screen limits.

None of them name what the choice actually says.

Here's what I think it says:

When a parent puts the phone down and looks at their child in an ordinary moment — not because it's a scheduled activity, not because they're documenting it — the child's nervous system receives something that no amount of enrichment programming can manufacture.

*You are the interesting thing. You are worth showing up for.*

This is identity formation at its most basic. Not through lessons or incentives or affirmations on the bathroom mirror. Through being noticed in ordinary moments by the person whose opinion matters most.

The research on attachment and identity is clear: children build their self-concept from what they perceive about their value to the people closest to them. Not from what they're told. From what they can feel.

A phone in another room says something.

Full attention in the kitchen at 3pm on a Wednesday says something different.

The "no phone summer" trend is parents instinctively reaching for something they know matters but can't quite name. What they're reaching for is this: the ordinary moments where their child finds out, without anyone having to say it, that they are worth the full attention of the most important person in their world.

That's not a screen-free summer. That's identity formation through presence.

And it turns out it doesn't require a plan. Just a phone in the other room sometimes, and eyes that look up when someone small enters the room.
