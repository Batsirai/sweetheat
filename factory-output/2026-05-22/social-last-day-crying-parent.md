I made it through the whole morning.

Through breakfast. Through the backpack check. Through the front-steps photo I take every year — first day, last day — so I can put them side by side later and see how much happened while I wasn't looking.

I even made it through the hug.

Then I sat in the car and cried.

Not sad exactly. Not happy. Something in between — the particular weight of a chapter closing that was good, that you're not ready to be done with, even though you know it was supposed to end.

If you did this today — or last year, or the year before — I need you to hear something:

You are not being dramatic.

You are paying attention.

And those are exactly the same thing.

You don't grieve what didn't matter. You don't feel the weight of something that cost you nothing. The fact that today lands — in your throat, in your chest, in the quiet car — is evidence that the year was real. That your child was real. That the growing you watched, the things you cheered for, the mornings you sent them out the door and hoped it would be a good day — all of it mattered.

Your tears are your nervous system confirming:

*That mattered.*

The year didn't stay in the building. Everything real about it came home in the backpack. In the child walking toward you this afternoon.

You were paying attention all year.

Today is just what that looks like from the outside.

## LinkedIn Post

Something happened in school pickup lines across the country this week that doesn't show up in any curriculum assessment.

Parents cried.

In cars. In parking lots. Quietly, so the other cars couldn't see. Not dramatically — just the quiet weight of a chapter closing that meant something.

I've been thinking about why that moment lands so hard, even for parents who aren't criers, even for the ones who are generally fine with change.

Here's what I think it is:

You spend nine months watching a person become something — braver, more articulate, more themselves — and then on a Thursday afternoon in May, the container that held all that growing closes. The teacher moves on. The desk gets assigned to someone else. The year becomes past tense.

And your nervous system, which has been tracking your child's becoming all year without being asked to, marks what mattered.

The end-of-school cry is not about the classroom. It's about what you watched happen inside it.

There's something in this for anyone who works with children, or raises them, or thinks about how identity forms in young people.

Belonging is not abstract. It lives in routines. In knowing where your desk is. In having a teacher who says your name like it belongs in the room.

When those routines end, a child's nervous system — and apparently a parent's too — knows. Not intellectually. In the body.

The kids who cry hardest at the last bell are the kids who loved best. The parents who sit in the parking lot afterward feeling something they can't quite name are the parents who were paying closest attention.

That's not a problem. That's the whole point.
