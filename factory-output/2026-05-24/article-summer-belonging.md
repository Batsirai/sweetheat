# What Your Child Is Really Asking for This Summer

*It sounds like "Can we go to the pool?" It isn't.*

---

The last day of school is a funny thing.

They're excited going in and something else coming out. Not sad, exactly — just slightly unmoored. Like a boat that's been tied to a dock all year and suddenly isn't anymore.

Leo came home on the last day and went straight to the backyard. No TV, no asking for anything. Just sat on the back steps with the dog.

His mom watched him from the kitchen window for a minute before she realized: he doesn't know what he is right now.

All year long, he'd been Leo-in-Mrs.-Garcia's-class. Leo-who-sits-by-the-window. Leo-the-kid-who-does-the-morning-announcements. He had a place. A role. A group that knew his name.

Now it was June. And none of that existed anymore.

## The Invisible Question Summer Asks Every Child

Here's what most parents don't realize about summer.

School doesn't just teach children reading and math. It teaches them where they belong. The classroom has a structure — a seat, a name on a cubby, a teacher who says "good morning" to them specifically, a group that waves them over at lunch. These aren't small things. They're the signals a child's nervous system uses to answer the deepest question it asks all day, every day:

*Am I somebody here?*

Summer removes all of it.

For six to ten weeks, the structures that told your child where they fit go quiet. No classroom. No cubby. No teacher. No routine that proves they're expected somewhere.

And nature abhors a vacuum. So your child — consciously or not — starts looking for those signals somewhere else.

They look for them at home.

---

## What "What Are We Doing Today?" Actually Means

If you've noticed that your kids seem to need more from you in summer — more reassurance, more proximity, more "Mom, watch this" — that's why.

It's not that they've suddenly gotten clingy or difficult. It's that the place where they used to get their daily answer to *am I somebody here?* has gone quiet for the summer. And they're running their usual search in the only place still open.

The question "What are we doing today?" is a logistics question on the surface. Underneath, it often means something simpler:

*Are we still together? Am I still yours? Do I still matter to you when nothing is requiring you to show up for me?*

You'll hear it in other forms too.

"I'm bored" sometimes means: *I don't know who I am without a place to be.*

"Can we call [friend]?" sometimes means: *I need someone who knows me.*

"Watch me do this thing" almost always means: *Tell me you still see me.*

None of this is a problem. It's attachment doing exactly what it's supposed to do.

## The Thing Summer Is Actually For

There's a version of summer that treats the months like a productivity gap to close — reading logs, enrichment camps, scheduled activities wall-to-wall to keep kids sharp and stimulated.

That's not wrong. But it misses the thing that only summer can give.

Boredom is where a child first meets themselves.

Not the panicked first-hour boredom, the kind that produces "there's nothing to do" on an infinite loop. The boredom that settles in on the third afternoon, when they've already watched everything and played everything and finally — finally — go outside with a stick and start building something they didn't know they were going to build.

That afternoon? That's where the self lives.

The long days, the slow hours, the unscheduled Tuesday in July — those aren't gaps in the summer. They're the whole point. They're where your child finds out what they actually like. What they think about when no one's telling them what to think about. Who they are when they're not performing for an audience.

Don't rescue them from the slow hours too quickly. The slow hours are doing something important.

## What You Can Actually Do (That Isn't a To-Do List)

This isn't about planning the perfect summer. Imperfect summers raise whole children too — maybe especially.

It's about knowing what your child is running their internal search for, so you can be the signal they're looking for.

A few things that land deeper than they look:

**Say their name when you don't need to.** "Hey, Noah" — not "Hey, come here." Not "Hey, stop that." Just his name, warmly, for no reason other than that you're glad he exists. Kids track this. They notice when they're named without a correction attached.

**Let them be boring with you.** Not every car ride needs a podcast. Not every dinner needs an activity. Sometimes just being in the same room, doing nothing together, is the most powerful signal: *I don't need you to perform. I just want you here.*

**Reflect who they are.** Not what they did — who they are. "You were so gentle with that little kid at the pool." "You're the kind of person who notices when someone's left out." Give them language for their own character. That language will come back to them in September when they need it.

**Answer the question they're actually asking.** When they need you to watch, watch. When they want to show you something, look up from the phone. These aren't interruptions. They're the search running. And every time you respond, you answer: *Yes. You are somebody here. Absolutely somebody.*

## The Summer That Stays With Them

The summer your child will actually remember isn't the one with the best itinerary.

It's the one where they felt like themselves. The one where the house felt like a soft landing. The one where they came back to you with something they made or said or figured out, and you acted like it was worth showing you — because it was.

That's the summer that becomes part of who they think they are.

Emma is twelve now. She still talks about the summer she was seven — the one where it rained for two weeks straight and her mom let her set up a whole store in the living room and acted like a customer every single day. The store sold rocks and drawings and things she'd found in the yard.

Emma's mom doesn't remember half of those "store" visits. She was half-listening while doing other things. She didn't know she was doing something that would last.

That's usually how it works.

You don't have to be perfect at this. You just have to keep answering: *Yes, I see you. Yes, you belong here. Yes, you are already somebody — before the next school year, before you earn anything, before you perform a single thing.*

That's what summer is actually for.
