## INSTAGRAM CAPTION

The last day of school is a funny thing.

They come home and something's different. Not sad — just slightly untethered. Like they're waiting for something to tell them where they are.

Here's what I didn't know until recently: school doesn't just teach reading and math. It teaches kids *where they belong.* There's a seat with their name on it. A teacher who says good morning to them specifically. A lunch table that waves them over.

Summer takes all of that away.

And without those daily signals, kids start looking for belonging somewhere else.

From you.

That's why "what are we doing today?" shows up fifteen times before 9am. That's why they need you to watch the same trick seventeen times. That's why they seem to need more of you this time of year, not less.

They're not being difficult. They're running their usual search in the only place still open.

And every time you look up and say *I see you* — even for thirty seconds, even with pasta boiling on the stove — you answer the question they're actually asking.

*Am I still somebody here?*

Yes. Always yes. That's the whole summer job.

## LINKEDIN POST

There's a pattern I keep coming back to when I look at what actually matters in childhood.

It's not the enrichment programs. It's not the summer reading logs. It's not even the family vacations — though those matter too.

It's this: whether the child knew, in their body, that they had a place.

Summer is interesting because it removes all the external structures that give kids that signal. No classroom, no cubby, no teacher, no lunch table. For six to ten weeks, the only daily answer to "do I belong somewhere?" comes from home.

That's a lot of weight for regular Tuesday afternoons.

The research on what children actually need for psychological resilience is surprisingly simple. They need at least one person who acts like they matter. Not in grand gestures — in accumulated ordinary moments. Looking up from a phone. Saying their name warmly, for no reason. Noticing who they are, not just what they did.

These moments aren't Instagram-worthy. They're hard to even remember later. But they're what a child's nervous system is tracking all summer long.

The book "The Whole-Brain Child" calls it co-regulation. I just call it being there enough that they don't have to keep checking.

It's the most important thing we do, in the most ordinary-seeming moments.

What's one small thing you do in summer that turns out to matter more than it looks?
