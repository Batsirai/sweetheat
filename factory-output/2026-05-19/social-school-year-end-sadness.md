## Instagram Caption

**[Image idea: quiet school hallway, or child looking back through a classroom window]**

My daughter used to cry on the last day of school.

Not a little sad. Like, *full tears in the parking lot* while I stood there with her backpack and a bag of craft supplies and absolutely no idea what to say.

I tried everything. "We'll have such a great summer." "You'll see your friends." "Third grade is going to be amazing."

She nodded through her tears and nothing landed.

Here's what I understand now that I didn't then:

She wasn't crying about the classroom. She was asking a question she didn't have words for.

*Am I still okay now that things have changed?*

The teacher who knew her name. The desk that was hers. The routine that told her what to expect every morning. Those were belonging signals. And when belonging signals disappear, a child's nervous system gets loud.

The answer to her question was never in the building.

It was in what she'd already heard about herself. The sentences that had already become the voice on the inside of her head.

*You're brave. I see you. You're the kind of person who tries again.*

Those don't evaporate when the last paper star gets taped to the door.

The sentences for that parking lot:

"It makes sense that you're sad. That was a good year."
"The best of this year is already inside you. You get to keep that."
"You still know who you are."
"Tell me your favorite part of the whole year."

Don't rush to the end. Let them talk.

## LinkedIn Post

The last day of school is in a few days.

And somewhere in a parking lot across the country, a child is going to cry.

Maybe yours. Maybe mine.

What I've learned — both as a parent and from the research on childhood belonging — is that the child who's saddest at year-end is usually the one who was most attached. Most alive to what they loved.

That's not a problem. That's the evidence of a good year.

But the parent standing in that parking lot doesn't always know what to say. We default to reassurance: next year will be great, you'll see your friends, summer will be wonderful.

None of that touches the actual question the child is asking.

Which is: *Am I still okay now that things have changed?*

The answer to that question lives in the sentences a child has already absorbed. The voice they've built on the inside from the one you've given them on the outside.

*You're brave. You're still you. You belong, not because of which classroom you're in.*

That's the foundation that moves with them from grade to grade, from school to summer, from childhood forward.

I wrote about this for AlreadyLoved — including the four specific sentences that help in that parking lot moment. Linked below for anyone navigating this week.
