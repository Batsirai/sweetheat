She handed me a card covered in crayon hearts — the ones that go off the edges, where she clearly ran out of space and just kept going.

And she climbed in my lap and said, completely matter-of-fact: "You're my best person."

## LINKEDIN POST

A few years ago I came across a simple idea that changed how I think about parenting content:

**Identity precedes behavior.**

The child who *knows* they belong doesn't need to act out to test it. The child who *knows* they're already loved doesn't spend their whole life performing for it.

This isn't a controversial claim. It's well-documented in developmental psychology — Bowlby's attachment theory, Neufeld's connection-before-correction framework, Neff's work on self-compassion. The consensus is clear: children who internalize early that they are unconditionally loved develop fundamentally different emotional architecture than those who didn't.

What's less discussed is the *mechanism*. It's not big speeches. It's not formal praise. It's thousands of ordinary moments — and what gets said in them.

**"What you say today becomes their inner voice tomorrow."**

That's the AlreadyLoved line I keep returning to. And it's especially true in the week before Mother's Day, when parents are in a reflective, emotionally available mood — already thinking about what their children mean to them.

We published a piece today that reframes Mother's Day: instead of asking what you want to receive, it asks what you want to *plant*.
