## INSTAGRAM CAPTION

There's a version of Mother's Day I used to want.

The quiet morning. The handmade card. Somebody bringing me coffee before I had to ask.

And I still want those things. But there's something I want more.

I want my kids to know — the kind of knowing that doesn't leave when I leave the room — that they are already loved.

Not because they're well-behaved. Not because they got good grades or said the right thing or made me proud that week. Just because they exist, and they are mine, and there is nothing they could ever do to change that.

Because here's what I've learned from seven years of parenting: kids are extraordinary interpreters. They watch everything. They narrate everything. And the story they land on isn't always the right one.

A distracted parent can look like a disappointed one. An exhausted mom can look like an unhappy one. And a child who isn't certain of their place — who has some quiet, unspoken question about whether they are fully loved — will start working for it. Earning it. Performing for it.

That's not a discipline problem. That's a belonging problem.

And belonging problems need one thing: more certainty.

So this Mother's Day, before anything else, I'm going to look my kids in the eyes and say the thing that I need them to carry.

*"I love you not because you're good. I love you because you're you. And you always will be."*

## LINKEDIN POST

Something changed for me when I became a parent, though I couldn't name it for years.

I kept trying to raise good children — children who behaved well, were kind to others, worked hard, said please. And I wasn't wrong to want those things. But I was wrong about the order of operations.

Here's what child development research has been telling us for decades: identity precedes behavior. A child who knows they are already loved doesn't need to perform to earn it. And a child who isn't certain of their belonging will spend enormous energy trying to secure it — through compliance, through acting out, through becoming whoever they think you need them to be.

The behavior we're trying to shape follows naturally from the identity a child holds. Get the identity right, and the behavior tends to take care of itself. Miss the identity, and all the rules in the world won't fix what's underneath.

This is the philosophy behind AlreadyLoved.

We make personalized children's books built on one idea: when kids know they're already loved — fully, unconditionally, before they do anything to earn it — they stop needing to earn it. And that's when something quietly powerful happens in them.

This Mother's Day, I've been thinking about what it means to give children the right inner voice while they're young enough to let it in. The words we say now go underground. They come back up at 16, at 34, at moments of self-doubt we won't be there to witness.

The best gift we can give the next generation isn't a toy that breaks or a skill they'll forget. It's a story they believe about themselves.

What you say today becomes their inner voice tomorrow.
