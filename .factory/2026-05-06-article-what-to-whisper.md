Here's how to use them.

## Why Mother's Day Is Actually for Them

Every year, the gift guides remind us what we deserve: the massage, the sleep-in, the brunch. And we do deserve those things — parenting is relentless work, and being seen and appreciated matters.

But the most lasting thing that happens on Mother's Day isn't what your family does for you.

It's what you do for the small person watching you.

Children are always watching. They are cataloguing how love works. They are storing up data about what it means to be in a family, what it means to be seen, what it means to matter to someone. Mother's Day is a day when all of that is heightened — when the rituals of love are made visible.

Which means it's one of the best days of the year to say the thing that needs to be said.

---

## What Children Are Actually Wondering

Underneath most childhood behavior — the testing, the clinging, the sudden declarations of "you're not my best mom anymore" — is a question that children can't quite articulate.

It goes something like this: *Am I loved even when I'm not easy?*

This is the deepest question of childhood. And most children never ask it out loud because they're afraid of the answer. So they test for it. They check for it. They behave in ways designed, consciously or not, to discover whether the love is still there when they push on it.

What children need — what they are always, on some level, hoping for — is the answer before they even have to ask.

They need to hear: *You are loved not because of what you do. You are loved because of who you are. And that doesn't change.*

## The Words (Save These)

You don't need to make a speech. You don't need a script. You just need to mean it, and say it, sometime in the next four days — on a regular morning, over cereal, or in the quiet right before they fall asleep.

Here are some versions of the same true thing:

**For the little ones (2–5):**
*"Do you know something? You are my very favorite you. And I love you just because you're you. Not because you're good. Not because you're clever. Just because you're mine and I'm yours."*

**For the middle kids (6–10):**
*"I want to tell you something, and I want you to remember it on the hard days too. You don't have to earn anything in this family. You already belong here. You always have. Nothing you do will change that."*

**For the tweens and teens (11+):**
*"I know things get hard between us sometimes. I know I don't always get it right. But I want you to know something that doesn't change no matter what: you are loved. Not for your grades, not for your behavior, not for anything you produce. Just for you. Just because you exist and you're mine."*

Say whichever version fits. Say it in your own words. They don't need to be perfect. They need to be yours.

## Why Saying It Out Loud Changes Things

There's a real psychological phenomenon that researchers call the inner voice — the ongoing self-talk that runs in the background of every human mind. For children, that inner voice is still being formed. It's assembled from what they observe, what they experience, and — most powerfully — what they are told by the people they love most.

The inner voice doesn't distinguish between an important speech and an offhand comment. It stores all of it. It files your words away and plays them back at 2am, in the middle of a hard semester, in the pause before a scary moment.

The question is only: which words are playing?

When a child hears consistently, across hundreds of ordinary moments, that they are already loved — not for their performance, not for their accomplishments, but for their being — that becomes the voice. The one that follows them out the door. The one that holds them when you can't.

That's not a sentimental idea. It's how identity is built.

## The Thing I Noticed When I Started Saying It

For a long time, I said the right things at the right times — at bedtime, after a hard day, when one of my kids was visibly struggling. And that mattered. But I noticed something when I started saying it in the ordinary moments too.

The ordinary moments are where it actually lands.

Not the tearful bedtime reassurance. Not the big talk after a hard week. The moment at the kitchen table when nobody is upset and nobody needs anything, and you just look at your child and say the thing. *Out of nowhere. For no reason.* Just: "I want you to know that I love you, not for anything you do. Just for you."

That's when they look at you funny. That's when you can tell it's going in.

Because when a child is already dysregulated — already scared, already in their feelings — the words are harder to receive. The nervous system is busy. But in a calm moment, a regular Tuesday, when they're eating their cereal and half-watching something and you're just there in the same room — the words can slip in without resistance.

They don't need a ceremony. They need a Tuesday.

---

## What Happens When They Start to Believe It

I've watched this happen in my own family, and I've heard it from other parents too.

There's a shift.

A child who used to check in constantly — "Are you happy with me? Did I do good? Are you mad?" — starts checking in less. Not because they stopped caring. Because they already know the answer. The certainty is built in now. They don't have to keep running the test.

A child who used to act out when feeling uncertain — the tantrums that came from nowhere, the defiance that seemed designed to provoke — becomes a little easier to reach. A little less defended. Because they're not spending energy fortifying against rejection. They're not bracing for love to be withdrawn.

A child who used to shrink starts to take up the right amount of space.

None of this happens overnight. It accumulates over months of ordinary moments, regular reassurances, small repetitions of the same true thing. Identity is not built in a single conversation. It's built the way a wall is built — one stone at a time, until there's something solid there that can hold weight.

But it starts somewhere.

It might as well start this Sunday.
