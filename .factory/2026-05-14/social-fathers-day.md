## LinkedIn Post

A 7-year-old asked her mother something that should stop all of us.

She had just scored her first goal. Her dad said he was proud of her. She was glowing.

Later that night, she asked: "What does Daddy think about me on the days I don't score?"

Not upset. Not insecure. Just... trying to map it out. Trying to understand the rules.

This is what children do. They're not passive recipients of our love — they're active interpreters of it. They listen to what we say, and they listen harder to what we *mean underneath* it.

"I'm so proud of you" is true. It's also love. But it's conditional love, and children can sense the condition even when we can't.

Research on secure attachment tells us something that should change how we talk to our kids: children don't need more love. They need the *question answered*. The question is always: *Am I loved regardless? Before the performance? Even on the worst days?*

When that question gets answered — definitively, repeatedly, in ordinary moments — children stop spending energy on it. They get to direct that energy somewhere else. Into trying hard things. Into building real friendships. Into becoming who they're supposed to be.

What does this look like in practice?

It sounds like: "I love you on your worst days most of all."
It sounds like: "You don't have to earn anything from me."
It sounds like: "I'm not watching to see if you win — I just love watching you."

These aren't affirmations to recite. They're truths to mean.

Father's Day is June 15. The most lasting gift a dad can give isn't something that goes under a tree. It's the knowledge — planted early, repeated often — that love isn't earned around here.

That knowledge travels with them. Into every hard year they'll ever face.
