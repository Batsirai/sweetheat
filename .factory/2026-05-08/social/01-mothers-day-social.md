## INSTAGRAM CAPTION

This Mother's Day is Sunday.

And if you're anything like me, you've thought about what to get — for yourself, from your kid, for a friend who just had her first baby.

But there's one question underneath all of it that I haven't been able to stop thinking about this week.

Does my child know they're already loved?

Not loved because they're sweet today. Not loved if they share their toys or sleep through the night or hold it together at the grocery store. Already loved. Right now. Just because they exist.

Because the world is really good at teaching children the opposite. That love is earned. That approval is won by being enough.

It doesn't say it out loud. It doesn't have to.

It's in every gold star. Every "good job." Every moment a child looks up — not with pride, but with a question: *did I do it right? Is that enough? Am I okay now?*

The research on this is quiet and heavy. Children build their felt sense of worth in the first few years. And that sense follows them everywhere — into every friendship, every relationship, every time they lie awake at 3am wondering if they're good enough.

We get to go first.

We get to say it before the world says otherwise.

This Mother's Day, I'm thinking less about gifts and more about words. What we say. When we say it. And whether our children are hearing it clearly enough to actually believe it.

## LINKEDIN POST

This Mother's Day, I keep coming back to one thing developmental science is quietly saying about early childhood:

The internal working model — a child's felt sense of whether they are worthy of love — is largely formed before age 5.

Not through grand moments. Through thousands of small ones.

The look on a parent's face when the child walks in the room. Whether their eyes light up. What gets said after a failure versus after a win.

Children who form a secure sense that love is unconditional — that they don't have to earn it — show measurable differences in resilience, emotional regulation, risk-taking, and relationship quality. Not because they were parented perfectly. Because the core question was answered clearly enough, early enough.

*Do I have to earn this? Or do I already have it?*

At AlreadyLoved, we build personalized children's books around this one idea: telling children, in their own name, that they are already loved. Not because they're good. Not if they behave. Already.

Because when children hear it clearly and early, the world has a harder time convincing them otherwise.

This Mother's Day, the most meaningful thing you can give a child isn't a toy. It's this belief, woven into something they'll hold and read and return to.
