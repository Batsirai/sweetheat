Nobody teaches a child that love is conditional.

Nobody has to.

They learn it from the thousand small calibrations of everyday life. From praise tied to performance. From warmth that appears after the good thing and disappears after the hard thing. From a world that is extraordinarily efficient at teaching children: you have to earn your place.

It doesn't start in adolescence.

It starts here, in the early years, when identity is still being formed.

And the thing is — children who grow up without hearing clearly that they are *already* loved tend to carry that question for a long time. Into friendships. Into relationships. Into the night when they're lying awake wondering if they've done enough, been enough.

I think about this a lot.

Because the alternative isn't "spoil them." The alternative is actually giving them *more* to work with. Children who feel securely loved are more resilient. More willing to try and fail. More capable of real connection.

Not because they were easier.

Because the core question was answered.

*Do I have to earn this? Or do I already have it?*

You get to answer that question. Early. Often. In the ordinary moments when there's no performance to reward.

## LINKEDIN POST

There's a moment in early childhood that most of us miss.

The child does something good. The parent says "good job." The child looks up — not with pride, but with a question: *Is that enough? Am I okay now?*

This is the moment the world's curriculum has already taken hold.

Children aren't taught that love is conditional. They infer it — from a thousand small signals about what earns warmth and what doesn't. From systems that reward performance and ignore the ordinary. From the cultural throughline in almost every story they hear: *be good, earn your place.*

Decades of attachment research (Bowlby, Ainsworth, Siegel) show that children who form a secure internal model of love — who genuinely believe they don't have to earn belonging — develop measurable advantages in resilience, emotional regulation, and relationship quality.

Not because they were parented perfectly. Because the core question was answered clearly.

At AlreadyLoved, we call this identity-first parenting. Identity precedes behavior. A child who knows they are already loved doesn't need to perform to earn approval — and paradoxically, they tend to behave better, try harder, and recover from failure more readily.

Because they're not spending their energy managing the question.

The question is answered.
