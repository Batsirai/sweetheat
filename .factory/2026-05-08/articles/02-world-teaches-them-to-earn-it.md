# When Kids Don't Know They're Already Loved, the World Teaches Them to Earn It

Emma was four years old when she started cleaning up her toys without being asked.

Her mom, watching from the doorway, felt that little swell of relief — *thank goodness, it's finally clicking.* She called out: "Good job, baby!"

Emma looked up. Not with pride. With something closer to a question. Her eyes were checking: *Did I do it right? Is that enough? Am I okay now?*

Her mom didn't see it. She was already back to making dinner. But that moment — the checking — was the beginning of something.

---

## The Unspoken Curriculum

Nobody sits a child down and says: *love is something you earn.* Nobody writes it on a whiteboard or sends it home in a newsletter.

But children are extraordinary learners. And they are learning, constantly, from everything around them.

They learn from the look on your face when they walk in the room. From whether you stop what you're doing when they say "Mama, watch." From whether they get warmth when they struggle or only when they succeed. From the thousand tiny calibrations that happen between a parent and child every single day.

They also learn from everywhere else.

From classrooms that reward compliance and punish disruption. From sports that track wins. From the slow creep of social comparison that starts younger than most of us want to admit. From the cultural message that runs through almost every story aimed at children: *be good, work hard, earn your place.*

It's not malicious. It's just the water we all swim in.

But here's what happens when a child absorbs this curriculum before they've absorbed the alternative: they begin to see love as conditional. As a thing that's given for performance and withheld for failure.

And that belief, formed quietly in the early years, tends to follow them.

## What Developmental Science Actually Says

There's a concept in attachment theory called the *internal working model* — a kind of psychological template a child builds in early childhood, based on their experience of being loved and cared for.

It's not about conscious thought. It's more like a felt sense: *do I belong? Am I wanted? Is the world safe for me?*

Mary Ainsworth's famous research in the 1960s and 70s showed that children who experienced consistent, responsive caregiving developed what she called "secure attachment" — a baseline confidence that they were worthy of love and that the world was basically safe.

Children with secure attachment weren't better behaved. They weren't easier. But they were more willing to explore, more resilient when things went wrong, and better at forming relationships. They recovered faster from hard moments. They were more willing to ask for help.

Because they didn't have to spend their energy managing the question of whether they were loved.

That question was answered.

---

## The Question That Has to Be Answered First

Here's what identity-first parenting is, stripped down to its simplest form:

Identity precedes behavior.

When a child knows — *deeply* knows, not just hears — that they are already loved, something changes in how they move through the world. They don't have to perform. They don't have to check. They can try things and fail at them and still feel fundamentally okay.

That security doesn't make them passive. It actually makes them more likely to take healthy risks, to work hard, to do the right thing when it's hard — because they're not doing it to earn love. They're doing it from a place of love that already exists.

This is what we mean by identity before behavior.

We talk a lot about what we want children to *do*: share, be kind, tell the truth, try hard. And those things matter. But the child who does those things because they feel loved and secure is growing differently than the child who does those things to earn approval.

## What the World Gets Wrong About Children

There's a particular kind of praise that sounds loving but isn't quite.

*You're such a good boy.* (Good compared to what? What happens when he's not?)

*I'm so proud of you.* (For this. For this specific thing you did.)

*Good job!* (Delivered after the performance. Withdrawn after the meltdown.)

None of these are terrible. We all say them. But they're behavioral praise — they respond to what a child *does*, not who a child *is*.

And children are listening very carefully for the difference.

What they need to hear, woven through the ordinary fabric of their days, is something different:

*I love you when you're hard to love.*

*You belong here, on the good days and the rough ones.*

*Nothing you do will make me love you more. Nothing you do will make me love you less.*

These aren't affirmations to recite. They're a posture. An orientation. A way of responding to a child that says: *your worth isn't something you're working toward. You already have it.*

## What Simmone Carried

Simmone grew up knowing how to be good.

She knew what got her mother's warmth. She knew what disappointed her dad. She learned the invisible rules early — be helpful, be cheerful, don't be too much, don't need too much — and she followed them carefully.

Nobody would have called her unloved. Her parents loved her deeply, genuinely. They just never thought to say: *this love is not something you earned. You couldn't lose it if you tried.*

So Simmone did what most of us do. She carried the question into adulthood, into friendships, into her marriage, into the way she looked at her own children and silently hoped she was doing enough.

It took a long time — and a therapist who asked the right question — for her to sit with the truth that had been there all along: she was already loved. She had been the whole time. She just hadn't heard it said clearly enough, early enough, for it to stick.

## What You Can Do Right Now

You don't have to be a perfect parent to be an identity-forming one.

You just have to say the thing out loud. Often enough that they start to believe it. Often enough that it becomes the voice in their head when you're not there.

Say it before they earn it. Say it after they've failed at something. Say it on a Tuesday when nothing happened and there's no reason for it except that it's true.

*You are already loved. Not because of what you do. Just because you're you.*

*I would choose you over and over again.*

*My love for you isn't something you can lose.*

Say it in the car. Say it at bedtime. Say it quietly when they're having a hard time and you don't know what else to say.

And find ways to let the world say it back to them too.

---

## The Word That Changes Everything

"Already."

Not "I'll always love you" — which is a promise about the future.

Not "I love you because" — which ties love to a reason.

*Already.* Right now. Before anything happens today. Before they're good or bad or easy or hard.

Already loved.

That's the word that changes things. Because a child who hears "already" hears something the world isn't teaching them: that love doesn't have to be earned. That they came into it. That it was there waiting.

The world will spend a lot of years teaching your child otherwise. You get to go first.
