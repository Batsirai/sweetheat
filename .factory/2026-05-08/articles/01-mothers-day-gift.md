# The Gift That Tells Your Child What They Need to Hear

There's something about May that makes you stop.

The school year is winding down. The days are longer. And somewhere between the glitter-covered card that came home in their backpack and the macaroni necklace they pressed into your hand, you find yourself looking at this little person and thinking: *do they know?*

Do they actually know how loved they are?

Not loved when they're good. Not loved when they share without being asked or sleep through the night or say please and thank you on the first try. Loved right now. Loved today. Loved with the crayon marks still on the wall and the meltdown still fresh from this morning.

That's the question underneath all the Mother's Day noise. And it turns out, it might be the most important one you ask.

---

## What We're Really Celebrating

Mother's Day has a way of becoming about mothers. Which makes sense — moms deserve honoring. But the older I get, the more I think about what motherhood is actually *for*.

Not just the sacrifice part. Not just the logistics and the worry and the meals that went cold.

It's this: you are, right now, building the voice in your child's head.

Every child grows up with an inner narrator. A quiet voice that tells them whether they're worthy. Whether they're enough. Whether love is something they *have* or something they have to earn by being good enough, smart enough, easy enough.

That voice is assembled slowly — from thousands of small moments. From the look on your face when they walk in the room. From whether your eyes light up when they find you. From what you say when they fall short, and what you say on the ordinary Tuesdays when nothing happened at all.

You are writing the script they'll hear for the rest of their lives.

---

## The Research Nobody Told You About

Developmental psychologists have a term for it: the *internal working model*. It's the lens through which children learn to see themselves, and it forms in the first few years, in relationship with the people who love them most.

Children don't just absorb facts about themselves. They absorb a *felt sense* of their worth.

Dr. Daniel Siegel, who has spent decades studying attachment and the developing brain, puts it simply: children need to feel *felt*. They need to experience being seen and known by someone who loves them — not for what they do, but for who they are.

When that happens, something shifts. Children who genuinely feel that they don't have to perform or prove or earn anything become more curious. More resilient. More open to being wrong and trying again.

They also become less likely to spend their adult lives chasing approval they never quite got as children.

---

## What Most Kids Are Learning Instead

Here's the quiet thing most of us aren't saying out loud.

The world is very good at teaching children that love is conditional.

Gold stars for good behavior. Timeout for the hard moments. Praise for achievement, silence for the ordinary Tuesday. And later — the comparison built into every classroom, every team, every app they'll eventually scroll through.

None of this is malicious. Most of it is genuinely well-meaning. But children are excellent at learning the unspoken rule: *I have to earn this.*

And once that's the internal script, it follows them. Into every friendship and relationship and workplace. Into the night when they're lying awake wondering if they're good enough. Into the morning when they get up and try a little harder.

It doesn't start in adolescence. It starts here, in the ordinary days of early childhood, when identity is still being formed.

## What to Say When You Give It

The book does work. But so do you.

When you sit down together, when you open it and read their name in a story about being already loved, you can say the thing out loud too.

*You don't have to earn this. You already have it.*

*I would choose you over and over again, exactly as you are.*

*My love for you isn't something you can lose.*

These aren't lines from a parenting script. They're the things children need to hear so many times that they start to believe them. So many times that the voice in their head begins to sound like yours — warm and steady and absolutely sure of them.
