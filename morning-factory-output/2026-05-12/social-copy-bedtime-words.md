## INSTAGRAM CAPTION

Those last five minutes.

After the books. After the water. After the thing where they suddenly have three questions about something that happened three weeks ago.

When the room finally gets quiet and they're looking up at you and you're both just... there.

I used to rush through that part. I'd say goodnight and sneak out like I was escaping a crime scene, already thinking about the dishes and the thing I forgot to do and whether I'd get more than six hours if I fell asleep right now.

But I've been thinking a lot about what developmental psychologists say about those moments — the ones right before a child falls asleep. Their brains are still building the architecture of who they believe they are. And in that quiet, whatever they hear has a different weight than it does at noon.

What you say today becomes their inner voice tomorrow.

Not the big speeches. Not the teachable moments. The thing you say in the dark when you think they're almost asleep.

So I started trying to say one thing true before I left the room. Not a script. Not affirmations I found on Pinterest (no offense to Pinterest). Just something I actually meant.

*You are so wanted here.*
*I see how hard you tried today.*
*There is nothing — nothing — you could do that would make me love you less.*

Some nights she just nods and drifts off. Some nights she grabs my hand and holds it.

You don't have to do this perfectly. You just have to do it sometimes. One sentence. In the dark. That's the whole thing.

## LINKEDIN POST

Most leadership development programs I've seen start with skills.

Communication. Strategic thinking. Resilience. The ability to perform under pressure.

But here's what child development research has quietly been saying for decades: most of these capacities — or their absence — trace back to something much earlier. Not to training. To the story a person was told about who they are when they were small enough to believe it completely.

Children's self-concept forms between roughly ages 2 and 10. Psychologists call this "narrative identity" — the story we tell ourselves about who we are and what we're capable of. And in that critical window, it isn't achievement that shapes the story. It's the messages children receive about whether they belong, whether they're capable, and whether they are loved without condition.

The research on secure attachment shows the same thing: kids who grow up knowing they are fundamentally okay — not because of what they do, but because of who they are — are more resilient, more curious, and more able to tolerate failure without it becoming a referendum on their worth.

We build leaders the hard way — training, coaching, 360 feedback — when the foundation was either laid or left unfilled decades earlier.

This isn't a business post. It's a parenting one. But these things are not separate.

What we say to our kids at bedtime eventually walks into our conference rooms.

I wrote something this week about the specific words that land differently — that name identity rather than just praise behavior. The article is for parents, but the principle is for everyone who believes that who people believe they are shapes what they become.

Worth a read, especially if you have small people at home.
