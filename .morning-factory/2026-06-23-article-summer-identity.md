# Summer Is the Best Time to Remind Your Child Who They Are (Before School Does It First)

*Format: Blog | Word count: ~1,700 | Target: AlreadyLoved Kids*

---

**Meta description:** Summer is the season when school labels fall away — and parents get to be the first voice. Here's what to say when nothing is expected of your child.

---

There's a particular kind of quiet that settles over the house in late June.

The backpack is in a corner somewhere. The lunch box has been washed and forgotten. The homework routine, the reading logs, the classroom behavior charts — all of it is just... gone for a while.

And in that quiet, you might notice something. Your child is just there. Not performing. Not being evaluated. Just being.

Maybe your daughter is in the backyard talking to a caterpillar for twenty minutes. Maybe your son is building something elaborate out of blocks that will never be shown to anyone. Maybe they're bored in the way only children can be bored — genuinely present to how slowly time can move.

This is one of summer's hidden gifts. The labels fall away.

For a few months, they are not "the struggling reader" or "the social one" or "the one who has trouble sitting still." They are just themselves. Unsorted. Ungraded.

Here's the thing about that window: it doesn't stay open very long.


## What School Teaches Them (Without Meaning To)

Schools aren't trying to define your child's worth. Most teachers are genuinely wonderful humans doing hard work.

But school is a system. And systems sort.

By second grade, most kids know exactly where they fall in the reading groups. By fourth grade, they've started to build an identity around whether they're "good at math." By middle school, the labels have layers — athlete, class clown, quiet kid, the one who always knows the answer.

Some of those labels are true. Some are just sticky. And some become walls.

The problem isn't the labels themselves. The problem is when a child starts to believe that the label is *who they are* rather than *what they happen to do right now*.

There's a difference between "I'm bad at reading" and "reading is hard for me this year." There's a difference between "I'm the smart one" and "I love figuring things out." The first set closes. The second set opens.

Summer is when the first set gets to soften.


## The Season When You Get to Be the First Voice

You've been co-parenting with a school calendar for nine months.

Every weekday morning, your child went somewhere where other adults — wonderful ones, most of them — were also shaping who they believe themselves to be. You had evenings, weekends, and your own voice in the car. But school had them for forty hours a week.

Summer shifts that.

For the next two months or so, you're the loudest voice in the room again. You're the one who decides what gets named. What gets celebrated. What gets noticed.

This isn't a pressure — it's an invitation.

You don't have to design elaborate identity-formation activities. You don't need a curriculum or a summer reading chart or a growth mindset journal (though if those things bring you joy, carry on).

What children mostly need is to be *seen* by someone who loves them without an agenda.


## What "Seeing" Actually Looks Like

Seeing is simpler than it sounds.

It's noticing out loud. It's saying the thing you observe about *who they are*, not just what they did.

Not "great job on that drawing" — but "you sat with that for a long time. You really go deep into things."

Not "you were so helpful today" — but "that's just who you are. You notice when people need something."

Not "you were so brave at the pool" — but "you felt scared and you did it anyway. That's courage."

The distinction matters because the first version names a behavior. The second version names a *person*.

Behaviors are situational. Identity is portable.

When September comes and the classroom climate is harder, or a friendship gets rocky, or a test comes back with a grade that stings — what your child reaches for is the thing that lives inside, not on a report card.

That inside thing is being built right now, in the long ordinary days of summer.


## A Few Phrases Worth Saying Out Loud

These aren't scripts. They're more like invitations — ways of giving language to what's already true about your child.

**For the kid who's feeling lost without structure:**
"You know what I love about you? You figure things out. You always find your way."

**For the one who's been labeled "too much" at school:**
"I love how much you feel everything. That's not a problem. That's a gift."

**For the quiet one who goes unnoticed:**
"You see things other people miss. I've always loved that about you."

**For the one who struggled this year:**
"This year was hard. And you kept going. That's who you are — you don't stop."

**For the one who was praised for being smart:**
"The thing I love about you isn't how smart you are. It's how curious you are. Those are different things."

None of these cost anything. They take about fifteen seconds. And they have a longer shelf life than almost anything else you'll give your child this summer.


## The Thing About Identity and Behavior

Here's what doesn't work: telling a child to "be more confident" or "believe in themselves."

Identity doesn't start with behavior. It works the other way around.

When a child knows — really knows in their body — that they are loved and that they *belong*, behavior tends to follow. Confidence shows up not because they performed their way into it, but because they didn't have to.

This is why the affirmation matters. Not as a technique to produce a better-behaved child (that's manipulation, and kids can feel it). But as a simple act of witness. "I see you. I know who you are. You are already something."

The world will spend the next thirteen years of school asking your child to prove themselves. Summer is the season you get to remind them that they don't have to prove anything to you.


## One Small Practice

If you want to make this concrete: try one identity statement per day this summer. Not praise for behavior. Not encouragement for effort. Just a true thing about who your child is.

You'll probably feel a little awkward at first. That's fine.

Your child might roll their eyes or shrug and go back to their video game. That's also fine.

They heard you.

The research on this is consistent: what children believe about themselves is shaped significantly by the language of the people who love them most. What you say today becomes their inner voice tomorrow. Not instantly, and not perfectly — but over time, the voice you use becomes the voice they use on themselves.

That's worth fifteen seconds a day.


## A Soft Landing

Summer won't last. September comes, and with it comes all the sorting and labeling and evaluating that schools do.

But your child will walk back into that building carrying something you put there. A sense of who they are that doesn't depend on a grade, a teacher's approval, or a reading group.

You gave them that. In the ordinary Tuesday afternoons. In the car on the way to the pool. In the three seconds before you turned off the light.

*"I love you. I love who you are. You're already everything."*

That's not a small thing.

That's the whole thing.

### LinkedIn Post

I've been thinking about what school systems do to children's identities.

Not maliciously. Most teachers are genuinely remarkable. But systems sort. And by second grade, most children already know where they rank.

The research on this is sobering: children's beliefs about who they are and what they're capable of are largely formed by age 10. The voices that shape those beliefs most powerfully are the ones closest to them — parents, caregivers, the people who love them at the kitchen table.

Summer is an underrated season for this work.

For nine months, parents co-parent with a school calendar. Other adults are also shaping what children believe about themselves, for forty hours a week. Summer shifts that. For two months, parents become the loudest voice again.

The research on identity-first parenting suggests a few things:

**What doesn't work:** Praising performance ("you're so smart") creates fragile self-concept. Children who believe they are inherently "smart" avoid challenge because failure threatens identity.

**What does work:** Naming *character traits* rather than outcomes. "You figure things out" is portable. "You got 100%" isn't.

**The window is narrow:** By late August, the school machine gears back up. Back-to-school shopping, registration, supply lists — the whole apparatus of evaluation resumes.

What parents can do right now is simple. Notice your child. Name what you see. Not what they achieved — who they are.

"You go deep into things."
"You notice people."
"You keep going even when it's hard."

This isn't soft. It's foundational. The sense of self that a child carries into a hard year — a hard teacher, a hard friendship, a hard failure — comes from somewhere. It came from the people who named them before the world got the chance.

That window is open right now.

Use it.
