## Instagram Caption

Preschool pickup.

Your kid walks out last, face doing the thing. You know the thing.

You ask what happened.

"Nobody wanted to play with me."

And you feel it too — that old ache of being the kid standing at the edge of a circle. You want to fix it immediately. List their friends. Tell them it's not true.

But here's the thing I learned (the hard way, several times):

That's not what they need.

They don't need reassurance that the problem isn't real. They need someone to sit inside the problem with them. To say: *that felt really lonely. I see you.*

And then—not instead, but after—to say something that goes deeper than today.

"You are the kind of person worth knowing. Some days it takes people a little longer to see it. But it's always true."

Identity before strategy. Belonging before coaching.

That's the whole thing. That's what we come back to, over and over.

What you say in that parking lot? Goes in.

It becomes the voice they use on themselves when they're standing at the edge of a circle ten years from now.

Make it a good voice.

## LinkedIn Post

This one is for anyone who works with children, leads teams, or has ever felt like they didn't belong in a room.

There's a developmental moment around age 3-5 when children stop playing near other kids and start wanting to play *with* them. They start noticing who got picked. Who was left at the edge.

They begin to measure themselves.

And the parents who handle this best — the ones whose kids move through social rejection without it rewriting their story — aren't necessarily the most socially skilled coaches.

They're the ones who speak to *identity* before they speak to strategy.

Not: "Try asking them to play." (strategy)  
But: "You are worth knowing. Some days it takes people longer to see that." (identity)

The research is clear on this. Gordon Neufeld, Brené Brown, and attachment researchers across decades land on the same thing: a child who has a secure sense of belonging *in one relationship* can weather the turbulence of social exclusion without it becoming their inner narrative.

The belonging doesn't have to be everywhere. It just has to be somewhere.

This is the soil that all behavior grows from.

Identity first. Belonging first.

We apply it to parenting at AlreadyLoved. But it's the same principle that makes great managers, great teachers, great leaders.

People need to know they belong before they can become.
