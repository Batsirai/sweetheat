# The Love a Child Earns Is Not Love

I was sitting across from a woman at a coffee shop once—a friend of a friend, a mom of three—and she was describing her kids with visible pride.

"My oldest is so responsible. My middle one is the entertainer. And my youngest..." She paused. "He's still finding his thing."

And I watched something happen in her face as she said that. A small tightening. The look of a woman who didn't mean anything unkind by it, and also didn't realize she'd just given me a window into the architecture of her family.

Three kids. Three roles. One still auditioning.

---

## How Children Learn to Earn Love

We don't do this on purpose.

We don't sit down and decide: *this child will be the responsible one, this one will be the funny one, and the third will have to figure it out*. It just happens. Our attention goes where the behavior is brightest. Our praise follows what's easiest to see.

"You got an A!"  
"You made me laugh so hard."  
"You're such a good helper."

These aren't bad things to say. But they are all responses to *output*. Performance. Something the child *did* rather than something the child *is*.

And over time—this is the slow, invisible thing—children sort this out. They learn the rules.

*Love follows performance.*  
*Attention follows achievement.*  
*Being good at something is how you get to belong.*

This is what we mean when we say that love with conditions isn't love. It's feedback. And the child on the receiving end isn't developing a secure sense of self—they're developing a system for optimizing approval.

---

## What Happens in the Nervous System

The research here is quiet but consistent.

Children who experience love as conditional—even subtly, even in a home full of warmth—develop what psychologists call anxious attachment. They're more vigilant. They're monitoring more. They're tracking the face of the adult in the room for signs of approval or disappointment.

That vigilance is exhausting. It's also adaptive, in the short term. A child who watches for approval learns to be a good student, a people-pleaser, a hard worker. These things look good from the outside.

What's happening inside is different.

Inside, they're running a background process at all times: *Am I doing enough? Is this good enough? Will they still love me if I fail this test, say the wrong thing, don't perform?*

This background process doesn't go away when they grow up. It follows them into relationships, jobs, parenting. It becomes the inner critic that no amount of success ever fully quiets.

---

## Identity Before Behavior

Here's the alternative. Not the opposite of parenting, not the absence of standards or expectations. Just a shift in sequence.

*Identity before behavior.*

Before we respond to what a child does, we speak to who they are. Before we celebrate the achievement, we name the person.

Not: "I'm so proud of you for getting that A."  
But: "You worked hard on something that mattered to you. That's who you are."

Not: "You were so good today."  
But: "You have a kind heart. I see it in how you treated your brother this afternoon."

This sounds subtle. It is. But it changes what the child hears underneath the words.

The first version says: *your worth is tied to this output.*  
The second says: *you are someone specific, and your actions are an expression of who you already are.*

One asks the child to keep performing.  
The other frees them to just... be.

---

## The Inner Voice Gets Written Early

This is the part that woke me up when I first encountered it, and it doesn't stop being true.

Whatever we say to children—especially in the small, repeated moments, especially at bedtime when their brains are open and the day is settling—goes in.

It becomes the voice they use on themselves.

If the voice we give them says *you are loved when you perform*, that's the voice that will show up when they fail their first big exam, when they lose the job, when the relationship ends. That voice will say: *of course. You didn't do enough. You're not enough.*

But if the voice we give them says *you are already loved. Already enough. Already chosen*—

That voice shows up differently.

That voice doesn't disappear when they fail. It says: *this hurt. And you're still okay. You're still someone worth knowing.*
